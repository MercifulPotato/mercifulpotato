Item(by='BlueTemplar', descendants=None, kids=[25747207], score=None, time=1610444361, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25740606, text='&gt; It&#x27;s not &quot;censorship&quot; for you, me, or any other regular private entity to refuse to actively provide help for others&#x27; speech.<p>Section 230 was created when Twitter (and the likes) were much less important for public discourse.<p>I&#x27;m willing to bet that it&#x27;s going to be amended, just like the EU version of it was.<p>Consider this precedent :<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Marsh_v._Alabama" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Marsh_v._Alabama</a><p>&gt; The Court rejected that contention, noting that ownership &quot;does not always mean absolute dominion.&quot; The court pointed out that the more an owner opens his property up to the public in general, the more his rights are circumscribed by the statutory and constitutional rights of those who are invited in.<p>&gt; In its conclusion, the Court stated that it was essentially weighing the rights of property owners against the rights of citizens to enjoy freedom of press and religion. The Court noted that the rights of citizens under the Bill of Rights occupy a preferred position. Accordingly, the Court held that the property rights of a private entity are not sufficient to justify the restriction of a community of citizens&#x27; fundamental rights and liberties.')
