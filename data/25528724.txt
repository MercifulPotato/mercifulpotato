There are arguments for both sides.<p>It was turned into a literal science by Xerox, IBM, and Microsoft (through the 90s) that involved actual study of the human body, perception, haptics, material science, light, psychology, human biases and preconceptions, holistic purpose, advances in micro mechanical engineering, usability studies, panel testing, and actual research (for just one example out of thousands, read the story behind the creation of the trackpoint [0]). Then different types of people wearing different hats became involved in the decision making process and the target consumer base shifted in a certain direction and “science” didn’t cut it any more.<p>Modern UI&#x2F;UX is absolutely not driven  by any science apart from sales conversion rates.<p>[0]: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;archive.is&#x2F;S4ESh" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;archive.is&#x2F;S4ESh</a>')
