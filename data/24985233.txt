Item(by='romanoderoma', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1604446439, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24983082, text='Not disagreeing with you either, but as an Italian it&#x27;s not the C, it&#x27;s the combination of C and other letters after it.<p>Cane (dog) and cena (dinner) in Italian are two examples of soft and hard C that depend on which vowel follows the letter C.<p>It wasn&#x27;t like that originally, Roman C sounded always like K, Caesar was pronounced Kaesar (German&#x27;s Kaiser comes from it).<p>Than middle age came and things changed, but the pronunciation stayed consistent after that<p>C+a, C+o, C+u = hard C<p>C+e, C+i = soft C<p>So it&#x27;s not simply about &quot;what you write is what you get&quot; but it&#x27;s the rule that is consistently the same<p>Phonetically the examples above are unambiguous though<p>Soft c = voiceless postalover affricate<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Voiceless_postalveolar_affricate" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Voiceless_postalveolar_affri...</a><p>hard c = Voiceless velar plosive<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Voiceless_velar_plosive" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Voiceless_velar_plosive</a><p>They are not exactly what you write, but they have precise phonetic symbols expressing how they sound (which is not always how they look on paper)')
