Item(by='DiogenesKynikos', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1608469426, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25484962, text='I think you&#x27;re actually making OP&#x27;s point: free laborers were capable of doing the same work that unfree laborers had been forced to do.<p>This isn&#x27;t my field, but I have read a few papers about the question of how profitable slavery was. Most of the debate seems to be over how profitable it was <i>to the plantation owners</i>, and whether they personally would have made larger profits by investing their money elsewhere (e.g., by hiring wage laborers to work their plantations, or by investing in manufacturing).<p>The question of slavery&#x27;s impact on the development of the economy as a whole is completely different from that. Given how backwards the South remained, while the half of the country that abolished slavery surged ahead economically, it&#x27;s difficult to see how one could argue that slavery caused the US&#x27; economic development.<p>Alexis de Tocqueville wrote about this in <i>Democracy in America</i>. It&#x27;s well worth reading, to see a contemporary Frenchman&#x27;s impressions of the stark difference in economic development between the Southern slave and Northern free societies: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.tota.world&#x2F;article&#x2F;1860&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.tota.world&#x2F;article&#x2F;1860&#x2F;</a>')
