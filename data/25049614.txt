Item(by='smnrchrds', descendants=None, kids=[25051612], score=None, time=1605034284, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25048285, text='You know how the US forced all states to increase drinking age to 21? That&#x27;s how Canada forced all provinces to adopt universal healthcare. Essentially, there is a minimum set of requirements the provincial healthcare plans must offer. They are free to not adhere to it, but then the amount of funds they get through Canada Health Transfer (money from the federal government given to provincial governments to spend on healthcare) will get reduced or completely removed. Canada Health Transfer accounts for approximately 10% of all provincial government revenues, at least in Alberta and Ontario that I checked. It&#x27;s a serious bit of change and a strong incentive to follow federal guidelines.<p>As a side note, the fact that the federal government can use its spending powers to enforce policy that is not within its jurisdiction is very unpopular in Quebec and one of the changes Quebec wants made to the constitution.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;budget.ontario.ca&#x2F;2019&#x2F;chapter-3.html#section-6" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;budget.ontario.ca&#x2F;2019&#x2F;chapter-3.html#section-6</a><p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;globalnews.ca&#x2F;news&#x2F;3496355&#x2F;quebec-canada-constitution-amend-reopen&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;globalnews.ca&#x2F;news&#x2F;3496355&#x2F;quebec-canada-constitutio...</a><p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;ualawccsprod.srv.ualberta.ca&#x2F;2019&#x2F;07&#x2F;federal-spending-power&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;ualawccsprod.srv.ualberta.ca&#x2F;2019&#x2F;07&#x2F;federal-spendin...</a>')
