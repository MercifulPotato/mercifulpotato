Item(by='myWindoonn', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1611078376, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25830466, text='Actually, yes, large companies often interfere in foreign governments&#x27; affairs. For a good recent example involving Facebook, consider the situation in Myanmar. Eric Schmidt, a revolving-door executive who connected Google&#x2F;Alphabet to the federal government, went to Myanmar in 2013 as they were transitioning from a junta to a democracy [0] in order to advocate that they adopt free and open Internet access [3]. That year, Myanmar allowed foreign telecommunications companies to operate, dropping the price of SIM cards by a hundredfold [1]. Facebook went live in Myanmar that year too, and enabled the junta to start spreading racist memes. Violence started to spread, and by 2016, an ongoing government-supported genocide of the Rohingya had started [2].<p>I am not saying that tech companies desired a genocide, but I am saying that their policies, platforms, and interference in foreign affairs are complicit in genocide.<p>[0] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;2011%E2%80%932015_Myanmar_political_reforms" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;2011%E2%80%932015_Myanmar_poli...</a><p>[1] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bbc.com&#x2F;news&#x2F;blogs-trending-45449938" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bbc.com&#x2F;news&#x2F;blogs-trending-45449938</a><p>[2] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Rohingya_genocide" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Rohingya_genocide</a><p>[3] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Eric_Schmidt#Advocating_open_Internet_use_in_Myanmar" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Eric_Schmidt#Advocating_open_I...</a>')
