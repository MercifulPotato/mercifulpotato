Item(by='Someone', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1609139982, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25555262, text='You need more broadcast power the further you’re away from earth and there’s less solar energy per m² the further you’re away from the sun (Neptune is 30 AU away, so there, per m² it’s about a thousandth of what we get around earth), and launching nuclear powered space probes (let alone a continuous stream of them) is a bit controversial.<p>Because of that, I think high bandwidth is problematic (Voyager 2 had 115 kbits&#x2F;second at Jupiter (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Voyager_2#Communications" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Voyager_2#Communications</a>) and currently has 160 bits per second; new Horizons, launched in 2006 has _less_ than that (smaller parabolic dish; less power ; dedicating fewer antennas on earth to receive data (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;space.stackexchange.com&#x2F;questions&#x2F;27450&#x2F;why-is-new-horizons-data-transmission-speed-so-slow-compared-to-voyager" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;space.stackexchange.com&#x2F;questions&#x2F;27450&#x2F;why-is-new-h...</a>))<p>You also would need a lot of large radio antennas on&#x2F;around earth if you want to receive from these probes 24h&#x2F;day (and if you don’t, you of course give up effective bandwidth)')
