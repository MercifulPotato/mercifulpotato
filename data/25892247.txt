Inevitably given the standard framework, such analyses did in fact find some depressing effect of growing trade on the wages of less-educated workers in advanced countries. As a quantitative matter, however, they generally suggested that the effect was relatively modest, and not the central factor in the widening income gap.</i><p>&gt; <i>Meanwhile, the political salience of globalization seemed to decline as other issues came to the fore. So academic interest in the possible adverse effects of trade, while it never went away, waned.</i><p>&gt; <i>In the past few years, however, worries about globalization have shot back to the top of the agenda, partly due to new research, partly due to the political shocks of Brexit and Trump. And as one of the people who helped shape the 90s consensus – that the income distribution effects of rising trade were real but modest – it seems appropriate to ask now what we missed. What aspects of rising trade did we either fail to see at the time or fail to anticipate?</i><p>* <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.gc.cuny.edu&#x2F;cuny_gc&#x2F;media&#x2F;liscenter&#x2F;pkrugman&#x2F;pk_globalization.pdf" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.gc.cuny.edu&#x2F;cuny_gc&#x2F;media&#x2F;liscenter&#x2F;pkrugman&#x2F;pk_...</a><p>One hour talk by him from 2019:<p>* <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=rWQ3jCURzy0" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=rWQ3jCURzy0</a><p>* <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;fbe.unimelb.edu.au&#x2F;newsroom&#x2F;paul-krugman-globalisation" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;fbe.unimelb.edu.au&#x2F;newsroom&#x2F;paul-krugman-globalisati...</a>')
