Item(by='rlpb', descendants=None, kids=[25311063], score=None, time=1607134711, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25310549, text='In my view, the retailer isn&#x27;t even legally at fault. Oculus is, for modifying the your device such that you can no longer use it.<p>Imagine buying a chair. A year later, the carpenter who made it lets themselves into your home and modifies it so it has three legs instead of four. They say it&#x27;s an improvement because it&#x27;s lighter now and easier to move around. But your elderly parent can&#x27;t use the chair any more because it&#x27;s less stable. Despite both arguments being valid, ultimately they had no right to modify your device.<p>This is a Tort (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Tort" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Tort</a>).<p>In the US, they&#x27;d probably be able to argue that there&#x27;s some ToS that permitted this. In most of the rest of the world, I&#x27;d argue that there was no ToS at the time of the sale, and that therefore it cannot be valid (especially as there was no consideration (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Consideration" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Consideration</a>) associated with that modification).')
