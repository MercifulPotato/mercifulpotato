Item(by='dsr_', descendants=None, kids=[24865154], score=None, time=1603390274, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24859375, text='I don&#x27;t know what you mean by &quot;because of the physics involved&quot;.<p>Suppose you send a 440Hz square wave, amplitude -1 to +1 V, to a speaker, and use a top-quality microphone and a reasonable oscilloscope to look at the resulting wave in the air.<p>Here are perfectly normal things that you can expect to observe:<p>- the wave is no longer square<p>- the beginning of each cycle (attack) is louder than the middle<p>- the middle of the cycle is louder than the attack<p>- the attack and decay are both ringing with new high frequencies that you didn&#x27;t send in<p>- integer multiples of the frequency are audible<p>- the sympathetic vibration of the speaker enclosure is nearly as loud as the signal<p>- it&#x27;s very good as long as you hold the mic in exactly the right spot, but if you move it you get different distortions<p>- the speaker catches on fire (this excludes plasma speakers, which are supposed to be on fire when active)<p>All of these things are because of physics.')
