They may exhibit some editorial lean (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.dw.com&#x2F;en&#x2F;a-dual-broadcasting-system&#x2F;a-435426" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.dw.com&#x2F;en&#x2F;a-dual-broadcasting-system&#x2F;a-435426</a>), but from everything I&#x27;ve seen not even to the level that folks in the US complain about CNBC or Fox News.<p>Germany, in fact, comes in with one of the highest press freedom scores from Reporters without Borders:<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;rsf.org&#x2F;en&#x2F;germany" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;rsf.org&#x2F;en&#x2F;germany</a> , <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;mediabiasfactcheck.com&#x2F;germany-media-profile&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;mediabiasfactcheck.com&#x2F;germany-media-profile&#x2F;</a><p>National public Radio and the Public Broadcast Company in the US, in further illustration, hasn&#x27;t been Donald Trump&#x27;s megaphone.<p>Just generally, independent journalism is important and can be subverted whether the sources are public or private. Open pluralistic oversight is most important to maintaining balance and Germany seems to get it right, while also making sure the media can be accessible to any resident.<p>Seems a not-awful approach to trying to ensure a more-educated population, which is also good for a democracy.')
