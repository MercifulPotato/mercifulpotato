He deliberately installed it on an ASG, <i>which is designed to drop instances</i>.<p>Using an ASG to manage a production database is a tale from the land of What The Everloving Fuck Were You Thinking, as you note, the author makes a habit of bad practices.<p>It&#x27;s at least instructive to note that there is only one canonical good practice when working with AWS:<p><pre><code>    Always assume you are handling a footgun until you have proven you aren&#x27;t.\n</code></pre>\nFor instance, you might think that Cloudformation is a reasonable way to manage a database in RDS because it&#x27;s an integrated service and you can keep your configuration in source. That must be a good practice, right?<p>While they have improved the AWS::RDS resource in that there are fewer ways in which small configuration changes will drop your database, there are still big red banners in the documentation.[1]<p>To do it, you must specify a policy to say, &quot;please don&#x27;t nuke my prod database.&quot;<p>Because AWS will always default to being a footgun.<p>[1]: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.aws.amazon.com&#x2F;AWSCloudFormation&#x2F;latest&#x2F;UserGuide&#x2F;aws-properties-rds-database-instance.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.aws.amazon.com&#x2F;AWSCloudFormation&#x2F;latest&#x2F;UserGui...</a>')
