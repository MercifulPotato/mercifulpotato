Item(by='snickell', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1607992275, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25425218, text='I agree with the assertion that the core &quot;hard tech&quot; in K8S, docker, Ansible, etc has been with us a long time, and some of us did indeed use it.<p>That said, knowing what to mix and match to create a &quot;pattern that you can learn and everyone uses and learns too&quot; does contribute value, its essentially parallel to the value provided by linux distros.<p>I can &quot;docker pull&quot; from many different distros, with lots of premade apps.<p>I think the lesson for the &quot;hard technology&quot; folks is when you solve a hard technical problem DO think about setting up and promoting high-level standards with sane defaults. You are the most knowledgable person(s) for your &quot;hard tech problem&quot;, and thereby often in the best position to standardize the default pattern by which it can be used.<p>That can contribute as much or more value than the hard technical work itself.<p>EDIT: also, don&#x27;t forget to publish and promote STANDARDS giving a well documented &quot;if you don&#x27;t have a reason not to, do it this way&quot; path for integrating useful tools together. That&#x27;s essentially what docker is&#x2F;did, and even though it can be recreated in a 100 lines of bash (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;github.com&#x2F;p8952&#x2F;bocker" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;github.com&#x2F;p8952&#x2F;bocker</a>) the branding led to ~100,000 developers publishing containers that are fairly easy for anyone else to understand and build from.')
