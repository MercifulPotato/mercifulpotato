Item(by='JumpCrisscross', descendants=None, kids=[25919640, 25919253], score=None, time=1611682373, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25917612, text='&gt; <i>I&#x27;m curious what evidence there is on either side of this claim</i><p>Looking at the list of the most deadly non-tropical infectious diseases [1], most can be vaccinated against and&#x2F;or cured. The standouts remain lower respiratory infections, <i>e.g.</i> influenza, and HIV&#x2F;AIDS.<p>When confronted with a novel coronavirus, it took a few months for the world&#x27;s medical systems to devise various treatments and a vaccine. Neither of those are recurring revenue streams.<p>Most damning to this conspiracy theory is the recent Hep C cure. That&#x27;s a real disease. It was profitable to treat. But it&#x27;s more profitable to cure. Those incentives remain elsewhere. A cure for HIV is worth billions.<p>Dealing with mortality and sickness is difficult. It&#x27;s more comforting to some to imagine an evil cabal holding back medicine, and I don&#x27;t need to take that from them. But if you&#x27;re entertaining these thoughts as anything more than a coping mechanism, the last half century--or even decade--of progress in curing, not treating, curing a variety of medical issues has been under-reported (lots of niche illnesses) and breathtaking.<p>[1] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Infection" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Infection</a>')
