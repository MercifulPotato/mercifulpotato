Item(by='digital-cygnet', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1603716942, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24895080, text='This is one of the few articles that violates Betteridge&#x27;s Law of Headlines[1], in that the answer is actually a qualified yes.<p>This is a topic that is often touted by the FIRE movement, but one I feel could use more mainstream understanding: very small decisions (ones that get washed out quickly by hedonic adaptation[2]), summed over a lifetime, can be the difference between comfortable financial independence and a cash-strapped retirement.  The rule of thumb that I&#x27;ve found helpful is to remember every $5 daily decision I make not to spend, if I conceptualize it as being made for my future self in similar situations on future days, adds up to $20,000 in a decade.<p>The mental exercise ends up being almost Kantian[3], but in a population of one: &quot;if I buy this latte, I am espousing the general principle that when I am bored and near a Starbucks I buy a latte. That habit costs $10k per decade. Is that worth it to me?&quot;<p>Of course, there is a place for treating oneself and differences in people&#x27;s saving habits are not the sole determinant of wealth outcomes because of a variety of other structural factors.  But I&#x27;ve found that treating habitual decisions this way has been a great boost in my mindfulness and pursuit of financial independence.<p>[1]<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Betteridge%27s_law_of_headlines" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Betteridge%27s_law_of_headli...</a><p>[2]<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Hedonic_treadmill" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Hedonic_treadmill</a><p>[3]<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Categorical_imperative" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.m.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Categorical_imperative</a>')
