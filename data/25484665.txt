Item(by='qiqitori', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1608450394, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25483311, text='How much of that added density is used for what kind of performance and how much is used to prioritize efficiency is up to the chip designers -- for the M1 you get a couple cores that focus on performance and a couple cores that focus on efficiency, plus a lot of area for the GPU and a not insignificant area for the mysterious (to me at least) 16-core neural engine (aside: I wonder if e.g. the graphics pipeline is able to make use of that, as it seems like it should be able to perform matrix multiplications, and it&#x27;d be a bit of a waste if that just sat there most of the time).<p>So you&#x27;ll probably see some variation but each time a process is scaled down to 0.7x of the previous size, you&#x27;ll get smaller transistors that use less power individually and you could expect a &quot;40% performance boost for the same amount of power and a 50% reduction in area&quot; (according to <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;semiengineering.com&#x2F;5nm-vs-3nm&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;semiengineering.com&#x2F;5nm-vs-3nm&#x2F;</a>)<p>90 nm (2003) * 0.7 = 63 nm<p>65 nm (2005) * 0.7 = 45.5 nm<p>45 nm (2007) * 0.7 = 31.5 nm<p>32 nm (2009) * 0.7 = 22.4 nm<p>22 nm (2012) * 0.7 = 15.4 nm<p>14 nm (2014) * 0.7 = 9.8 nm<p>10 nm (2016) * 0.7 = 7 nm<p>7 nm (2018) * 0.7 = 4.9 nm<p>5 nm (2020) * 0.7 = 3.5 nm<p>To me, it&#x27;s a bit of a miracle that Intel is still able to sort of compete on mostly 14 nm nodes, but maybe that&#x27;s because &quot;node size&quot; basically just means &quot;smallest feature size&quot;, and their 14 nm or new 10 nm process is a little better than e.g. competing 14&#x2F;10 nm processes, or maybe their chip designs just prioritize different things that have a decent real world effect (e.g. Intel CPUs have AVX-512, but AMD CPUs don&#x27;t).')
