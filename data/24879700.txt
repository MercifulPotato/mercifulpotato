I&#x27;m saying that nuclear fission is indeed a totally zero carbon process, but the <i>life-cycle</i> emits 12 gCO2-eq&#x2F;kWh. The term life-cycle is a technical term from the academic concept of Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), which includes all the energy and emissions from the processes of mining, fabrication, construction, operation, and decommissioning.<p>The life-cycle numbers I give are sourced directly from an IPCC meta-analysis [2]. With nuclear, the entire life-cycle is extraordinarily low  carbon even though it requires lots of concrete to build because you get about a billion Watts 90% of the time for 60 years given that much concrete.<p>As for what&#x27;s easier, the common answer seems to be that variable renewables are ok for a while but at deep decarbonization, the situation is much easier and cheaper if you include some kind of low-carbon energy that can run 24&#x2F;7, aka a &quot;firm low-carbon source&quot; [3].<p>[1] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Life-cycle_assessment" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Life-cycle_assessment</a><p>[2] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.ipcc.ch&#x2F;site&#x2F;assets&#x2F;uploads&#x2F;2018&#x2F;02&#x2F;ipcc_wg3_ar5_annex-iii.pdf" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.ipcc.ch&#x2F;site&#x2F;assets&#x2F;uploads&#x2F;2018&#x2F;02&#x2F;ipcc_wg3_ar5...</a><p>[3] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;doi.org&#x2F;10.1016&#x2F;j.joule.2018.08.006" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;doi.org&#x2F;10.1016&#x2F;j.joule.2018.08.006</a>')
