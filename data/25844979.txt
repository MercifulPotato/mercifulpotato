Item(by='leroy_masochist', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1611140877, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25834804, text='Here&#x27;s what I don&#x27;t understand - this is is constantly framed as a matter of reforming Federal law, when the states could remedy this themselves by changing from a winner-gets-all paradigm to a Maine- or Nebraska-like system that allocates delegates proportionally.<p>The proximate reason why they don&#x27;t do this, I would imagine, is that in the short term a party-controlled state wouldn&#x27;t want a bunch of its Presidential vote delegates to go to the other party; e.g., California Democrats, who currently have the political power to change how their state awards delegates, don&#x27;t want dozens of delegates going to the GOP and vice versa for Texas Republicans not wanting a bunch of Texas delegates going to the Democrats.<p>On the other hand, states like Ohio and Florida want to keep the influence they have as swing states -- they want presidential candidates to spend an insanely disproportionate amount of time campaigning there and making promises about future policy decisions that disproportionately affect local industries. If they awarded delegates proportionally, they&#x27;d lose the attention they enjoy every four years.<p>I&#x27;m not sure what the way to break the equilibrium would be, but do think that the solution to this is action on the state level rather than Federal. Am I missing anything?')
