(Google Books:  <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;books.google.com&#x2F;books?id=LiQgAQAAMAAJ&amp;q=%22content+is+king%22&amp;dq=%22content+is+king%22&amp;hl=en&amp;sa=X&amp;ved=0ahUKEwi87Kaoq5brAhUYCs0KHdwWC2QQ6AEIMDAB" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;books.google.com&#x2F;books?id=LiQgAQAAMAAJ&amp;q=%22content+...</a>)<p>A discussion based on this premise should at least get the provenance straight.<p>Aguments over provenance notwithstanding, my view is that the aphorism is a convenient bit of stage distraction attractive to media monopolists themselves aware of the real truth:  <i>network control is emperor.</i>  Andrew Odlyzko argues this; <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;papers.ssrn.com&#x2F;sol3&#x2F;papers.cfm?abstract_id=235282" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;papers.ssrn.com&#x2F;sol3&#x2F;papers.cfm?abstract_id=235282</a> (as @fmajid notes elsewhere in this thread)<p>And this goes beyond just getting the proprietor&#x27;s cut, the vigorish, skimming your 5, or 10, or 30%.<p>Central control of a network means <i>deciding what that network is</i>.  Where it begins, where it ends, what goes in, what comes out, who can receive, who can send, what interactions are possible, speeds and latencies, what messages are heard, what are not, who pays, who gets paid, who plays for free.<p>These factors are wholly ignored by contemporary US (Borkian) antitrust doctrine.<p>It&#x27;s not just the vig.<p>Sumner Redstone died this past August at 97; \n (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.nytimes.com&#x2F;2020&#x2F;08&#x2F;12&#x2F;obituaries&#x2F;sumner-redstone-dead.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.nytimes.com&#x2F;2020&#x2F;08&#x2F;12&#x2F;obituaries&#x2F;sumner-redston...</a>) (NYTimes).')
