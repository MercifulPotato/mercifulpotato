Item(by='nobody9999', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1608583041, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25496725, text='From a layman&#x27;s (an interested one, at least) perspective, both MOND and LCDM seem to be lacking in some respects.<p>The idea that there exists some &quot;stuff&quot; we can&#x27;t measure (except weakly via observed gravitational effects) that is ~5-6 times the stuff we <i>can</i> measure and interact with seems a bit problematic.<p>Likewise, the idea that <i>fundamental</i> forces aren&#x27;t fundamental and function differently in different circumstances&#x2F;environments seems to contradict much of what we&#x27;ve learned&#x2F;deduced about the physical properties of the universe.<p>As such, it seems likely that our theories (in the scientific sense rather than the vernacular) are lacking in one or more respects.<p>Since these discrepancies focus on the effect we call gravity, I&#x27;d posit that until we can solve the Hierarchy Problem[0], there won&#x27;t be a clear understanding of why we see these anomalous effects.<p>That assumes we don&#x27;t find actual particles which fit the predictions of LCDM or clear evidence that the strength of gravity is dependent upon MOND principles.<p>Since many of the hypotheses that address the Hierarchy Problem are (at least currently) non-falsifiable, that&#x27;s a big issue.<p>I&#x27;m sure that in the above statements I&#x27;ve ignored various observed evidence (I am a layman, after all), but IMHO it doesn&#x27;t hurt to speculate.<p>[0] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Hierarchy_problem" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Hierarchy_problem</a>')
