Item(by='denotational', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1607182722, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25314070, text='I&#x27;m entirely incapable of understanding how a UK SLC &quot;loan&quot; can be considered a loan in the conventional sense of the word when it seems to have literally no characteristics of a loan, and every characteristic of a tax:<p>* You only pay it when you are in employment and earning money (with a minimum salary for repayments to be taken);<p>* The repayments are calculated as a function of your taxable income;<p>* The balance isn&#x27;t discharged by bankruptcy;<p>* The <i>balance</i> isn&#x27;t considered by lenders when assessing your suitability to take on &quot;real&quot; debt.<p>As far as I can tell, it&#x27;s a graduate tax (on those who took a loan) for all intents and purposes.<p>I see comments from students talking about being &quot;crushed by debt&quot; as a result of taking an SLC loan, and I just can&#x27;t work out where this perception comes from: yes, it decreases your take-home pay, but since there&#x27;s a minimum salary threshold for payments to be taken, it&#x27;s never going to leave you destitute.')
