Item(by='JohnBooty', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1608156534, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25445627, text='No, not remotely.<p>It may be true that a particular company has a stranglehold on browser choice <i>on your particular phone</i>, but that is completely different from having an anticompetitive strangehold <i>on the market as a whole</i>, which is what matters from an antitrust standpoint. I&#x27;m not the biggest fan of capitalism but the market seems to be functioning rather well. Not perfectly but well.<p>Just a refresher: IE had <i>96%</i> market share at its peak, and Microsoft were guilty of quite a few other anticompetitive practices as well w.r.t. Windows itself, more or less literally penalizing OEMs from shipping any other desktop OS.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Browser_wars" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Browser_wars</a><p>Competition is thriving in the mobile space. Safari, Chrome, and derivatives of Chrome are competing with each other and they are well-performing, aggressively maintained and updated pieces of software.<p>Sure, there are plenty of technical reasons to be annoyed with either one of them. Mobile Safari sure is slow to adopt certain standards, etc. And don&#x27;t get me wrong -- it is extremely regrettable whenever browser choice is restricted. It sucks, I hate it.')
