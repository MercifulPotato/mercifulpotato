Item(by='tomp', descendants=None, kids=[24866510], score=None, time=1603386961, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24860280, text='Sure, I guess you&#x27;re right, if you consider formal grammars as existing in their own abstract bubble removed from our reality.<p>I, on the other hand, am primarily interested in using grammars to parse programming languages, which are essentially a <i>human</i> form of communication (computers could use bitcode or lisp-style ASTs, no need for syntax).<p>I&#x27;m not an expert on PEGs, so this is copy-pasted from Wikipedia page on PEGs, I hope it&#x27;s valid - the classical <i>dangling else</i> ambiguity.<p><pre><code>     S ‚Üê &#x27;if&#x27; C &#x27;then&#x27; S &#x27;else&#x27; S &#x2F; &#x27;if&#x27; C &#x27;then&#x27; S\n</code></pre>\nNow, there is an <i>obvious</i> ambiguity here, <i>for a human reader</i> (mediated, of course, by tabs). If you&#x27;re using a formalism that wishes that ambiguity away, it just means that the formalism is a non-ideal one. What I like about LALR parser generators is, that they will <i>explicitly</i> alert you of this kind of <i>human-level</i> ambiguities.')
