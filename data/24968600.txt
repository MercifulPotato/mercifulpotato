The full article is at <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;jan.rychter.com&#x2F;enblog&#x2F;cloud-server-cpu-performance-" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;jan.rychter.com&#x2F;enblog&#x2F;cloud-server-cpu-performance-</a>...<p>Your results (e.g. that z1d.xlarge with 4 vCPUs is only 10% slower than z1d.2xlarge with 8 vCPUs) shows that the &quot;performance&quot; you were testing was disk IO throughput (probably dominated by disk latency), not vCPUs.<p>&gt; My takeaways were that many cloud provider offerings make no sense whatsoever, and that Xeon processors are mostly great if you are a cloud provider and want to offer overbooked &quot;vCPUs&quot;.<p>&gt; I haven&#x27;t tested those specific setups, but I strongly suspect a dedicated server from OVH is much faster than a 4.16xlarge from AWS.<p>You seem to be implying that AWS&#x2F;EC2 does CPU over-provisioning on all instance types; this is incorrect, only T-family instance types use CPU over-provisioning.')
