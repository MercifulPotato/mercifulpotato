Item(by='vitus', descendants=None, kids=[25178675], score=None, time=1606024554, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25174832, text='&gt; Dr George Gheverghese Joseph from The University of Manchester says the &#x27;Kerala School&#x27; identified the &#x27;infinite series&#x27;- one of the basic components of calculus - in about 1350.<p>&gt; The discovery is currently - and wrongly - attributed in  books to Sir Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibnitz at the end of the seventeenth centuries.<p>Um, infinite series existed in the time of the ancient Greeks (even if they went out of their way to avoid using limits).<p>An instance of Archimedes dealing with an infinite geometric series: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;The_Quadrature_of_the_Parabola" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;The_Quadrature_of_the_Parabola</a><p>Here&#x27;s the thing, though: phenomena can be rediscovered, with discoveries spaced even hundreds or thousands of years apart (e.g. Pythagorean theorem).<p>And, there&#x27;s no strong evidence either way regarding Kerala school results 1) making their way to Europe in the 17th century or before, and 2) influencing results by Newton, Leibniz, and more.<p>But even if this is the case, Newton&#x27;s line about standing on the shoulders of giants is apt -- there&#x27;s no dispute here that he and Leibniz still had invaluable contributions that led to the development of calculus, or that they built on an existing foundation.')
