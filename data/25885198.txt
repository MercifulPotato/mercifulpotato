Due to network effects, there&#x27;s an argument that some types of online services are natural monopolies [0], and should be regulated the way we do for electricity, water, etc. There are reasonable arguments against such regulation (in any domain); but it&#x27;s potentially within the purview of the state to set &quot;rules of the road&quot; for the market, which in this context could mean digital due process, algorithmic transparency, interoperability, etc.<p>2. While I don&#x27;t disregard Barry Goldwater&#x27;s argument against the Civil Rights Act [1], the fact is that right now we <i>do</i> actively interfere in the marketplace to mandate transactions not be constrained by &quot;protected categories&quot;: race&#x2F;gender&#x2F;creed&#x2F;etc. I don&#x27;t think it&#x27;s at all unreasonable to proactively extend this protection to include political alignments or even epistemic opinions.<p>(One of the things I find most curious about CRA, is that while most of its categories are &quot;immutable characteristics&quot;, one&#x27;s faith is more a matter of choice than genetics. If I claimed my religion was &quot;I worship $POLITICAL_PARTY&quot;, would it then be protected? What is the scope of what defines a &quot;faith&quot;?
