I have most of this and various toolchains installed as standard on my work boxes, so for me, for instance, to get a preview pain in Atom, I just added 2 packages and the Atom module and it worked. For Pandoc, even less -- I just installed Pandoc and bingo, I could convert to&#x2F;from.<p>I feel sure that there must be some happy medium somewhere between the skeletal marked-up text formats of RST, ADoc and Markdown and the excessive complexity of (say) DocBook, but I don&#x27;t know what it is, and if it exists, it might not be FOSS.<p>I used to do a lot of writing and editing for Wikipedia (before someone unjustly accused me of vandalism, and a couple of my bigger pieces were deleted -- after that, sod them, I just do minor copy-edits) and I was happier with MediaWiki markup, but I guess it&#x27;s not very intuitive.<p>The professional FOSS documentation tools I&#x27;ve worked with are at one extreme -- the &quot;Docs as Code&quot; philosophy that holds that embracing programmers&#x27; tools such as Git and various programmers&#x27; editors mean getting a lot of power for little investment.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.docslikecode.com&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.docslikecode.com&#x2F;</a><p>The other extreme are powerful proprietary tools, often on Windows, such as MadCap Flare, which I haven&#x27;t worked with.<p>I reckon there is space in the middle for something like Wikipedia with versioning and branches, but nobody&#x27;s inclined to invest in the R&amp;D. There are tools, they work, so why should they?')
