Item(by='PurplePanda', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602682703, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24773357, text='The first sermon is said to be recorded by the dhammacakkappavattana sutta.<p>Ṭhānissaro Bhikkhu&#x27;s translations of the pāli Canon are usually sensible: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.dhammatalks.org&#x2F;suttas&#x2F;SN&#x2F;SN56_11.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.dhammatalks.org&#x2F;suttas&#x2F;SN&#x2F;SN56_11.html</a><p>or Bhikkhu Bodhi: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.budsas.org&#x2F;ebud&#x2F;ebsut001.htm" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.budsas.org&#x2F;ebud&#x2F;ebsut001.htm</a><p>There are also translations around that come via the Chinese cannon instead.<p>The suttas are probably very hard to understand without background knowledge or commentary (or, really, actual practice), but of course I don&#x27;t know which of the commentaries GP refers to. The dhammacakkappavattana sutta, being one of the important&#x2F;famous ones, has truly voluminous commentary from truly voluminous sources.<p>GP refers to &quot;continuous effort to face the uncomfortable&quot;. I&#x27;m not sure that I&#x27;d use that sentence to summarise the Buddhas teaching to be honest. But I suppose that it could be a reference to the 6th limb of the noble eightfold path (right-effort), or the 7th (right-mindfulness), or to the 1st noble truth (the truth of unsatisfactoriness&#x2F; suffering).<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Noble_Eightfold_Path" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Noble_Eightfold_Path</a><p>First sermon aside, his last words also, as given in the maha-parinibbana sutta, were to be &quot;heedful&quot; or to &quot;strive diligently&quot;.')
