Item(by='crazygringo', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602177564, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24721154, text='When a company is spun off, oftentimes the parent company still continues to own a minority stake, and&#x2F;or shareholders will wind up with shares in both.<p>It&#x27;s in nobody&#x27;s interest for &quot;betterIBM&quot; to succeed at the greater expense of &quot;crapIBM&quot;. With so much shared ownership (at least in the medium-term, practically speaking), shareholders want <i>both</i> to succeed.<p>That&#x27;s the whole point -- shareholders think both halves will do better as separate entities, and it&#x27;s in nobody&#x27;s interest for one half to exploit the other.<p>Surely your &quot;crapIBM&quot; will continue to have access to &quot;betterIBM&quot;&#x27;s tools at a reasonable price, but they&#x27;ll <i>also</i> be free to migrate to better ones, as they choose, at the pace that is most profitable for them.<p>It&#x27;s win-win because that&#x27;s the entire point of the split in the first place. The two resulting entities aren&#x27;t even competing with each other, they&#x27;re in totally different markets.')
