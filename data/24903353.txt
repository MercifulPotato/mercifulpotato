Item(by='dredmorbius', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1603759607, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24893866, text='<i>Universal&#x27;s sole textual argument is that fair use is not &quot;authorized by the law&quot; because it is an affirmative defense that excuses otherwise infringing conduct. Universal&#x27;s interpretation is incorrect as it conflates two different concepts: an affirmative defense that is labeled as such due to the procedural posture of the case, and an affirmative defense that excuses impermissible conduct. Supreme Court precedent squarely supports the conclusion that 1133</i>1133 fair use does not fall into the latter camp: &quot;[A]nyone who ... makes a fair use of the work is not an infringer of the copyright with respect to such use.&quot; Sony Corp. of Am. v. Universal City Studios, Inc., 464 U.S. 417, 433, 104 S.Ct. 774, 78 L.Ed.2d 574 (1984).*<p>-- Lenz v. Universal Music Corp., 801 F. 3d 1126 - Court of Appeals, 9th Circuit 2015<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;scholar.google.com&#x2F;scholar_case?case=12567649168680108221" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;scholar.google.com&#x2F;scholar_case?case=125676491686801...</a>')
