Item(by='segfaultbuserr', descendants=None, kids=[25289834, 25287163], score=None, time=1606986150, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25285619, text='I recently learned about the existence of the Prosthaphaeresis algorithm [0], an even more ancient and obsolete trigonometric calculation technique. It allows you to use a trig table to do calculations when you don&#x27;t have a log table. To calculate <i>a</i>  x <i>b</i>, find arccos(<i>a</i>) and arccos(<i>b</i>), then use the product-to-sum formula: cos(<i>a</i>) x cos(<i>b</i>) = 1&#x2F;2 x [cos(<i>a</i> + <i>b</i>) + cos(<i>a</i> - <i>b</i>)]. For example, to calculate 4294967296 x 2147483648. First, scale both numbers down by a factor of 1e10, <i>a</i> ≈ 0.4295, <i>b</i> ≈ 0.2147. Next, do inverse trig table lookup: <i>x</i> = arccos(<i>a</i>) ≈ 1.1269, <i>y</i> = arccos(<i>b</i>) ≈1.3544. Now, sum and difference: <i>y</i> + <i>x</i> = 2.4813, <i>y</i> - <i>x</i> = 0.2275. Finally, do trig table lookup and scale up, (cos(2.4813) + cos(0.2275)) x 1&#x2F;2 x 1e20 ≈ 9221026434613711000. The true answer is 9223372036854775808, error is less than 0.03%. It was used by astronomers in the 16th century before logarithm was invented.<p>I learned it while reading the Wikipedia article <i>Slide Rule</i>, it mentions that two math educators David B. Sher and Dean C. Nataro invented a Prosthaphaeresis slide rule in 2004 - it&#x27;s what a slide rule may look like in an alternative universe. Unfortunately, I still haven&#x27;t seen it - I couldn&#x27;t find the original paper, it doesn&#x27;t seem to have a DOI, can&#x27;t use Sci-Hub.<p>[0] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Prosthaphaeresis" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Prosthaphaeresis</a>')
