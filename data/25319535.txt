Item(by='pdm55', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1607212011, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25318387, text='I believe that Sicily, during the 1100s, was the place where translations from both Arabic and Greek to Latin occurred. In that century, it was ruled by the Normans who had conquered the Arabs. (Yes, the Normans, that same ex-Viking, Normandy-based group who were also busy conquering England, at that time!) There were separate law courts for each of the three language groups and translators who could speak all three languages.<p>I couldn&#x27;t find a definitive reference. There is this short scholarly article from 1920, <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.jstor.org&#x2F;stable&#x2F;1834924?seq=13#metadata_info_tab_contents" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.jstor.org&#x2F;stable&#x2F;1834924?seq=13#metadata_info_ta...</a>. And this series of articles about Sicily&#x27;s history, <a href="http:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bestofsicily.com&#x2F;history.htm" rel="nofollow">http:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bestofsicily.com&#x2F;history.htm</a><p>One key figure seems to have been Adelard of Bath. &quot;He is known both for his original works and for translating many important Arabic and Greek scientific works of astrology, astronomy, philosophy and mathematics into Latin from Arabic versions, which were then introduced to Western Europe.&quot; <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Adelard_of_Bath" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Adelard_of_Bath</a>')
