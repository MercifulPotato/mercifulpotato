Item(by='gxqoz', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1603129933, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24826873, text='I&#x27;m not sure if they&#x27;re using Python per se, but Netflix gave an interesting talk at KDD 2018 on how they&#x27;re using machine learning in various parts of film&#x2F;TV production.<p>One example is optimizing the workflow of a special effects director. They described how a typical person spends one day of their week reviewing scripts to determine which scenes are going to require CGI. They&#x27;re traditionally had to read every page in the script. Netflix built a model that filters scenes to just those that are likely to need effects, saving time.<p>Of course, it&#x27;s hard for me to independently verify the effectiveness of this model. If some false negatives get through (scenes that ought to have effects that aren&#x27;t noticed until too late), I can imagine an effects supervisor not trusting the model and just reading everything.<p>It doesn&#x27;t seem to have been recorded, but there&#x27;s the description: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.kdd.org&#x2F;kdd2018&#x2F;applied-data-science-invited-talks&#x2F;view&#x2F;jen-walraven" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.kdd.org&#x2F;kdd2018&#x2F;applied-data-science-invited-tal...</a>')
