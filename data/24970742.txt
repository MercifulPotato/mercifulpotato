Item(by='Bluestein', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1604338756, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24969552, text='We might as well ask Kuleshov about this, father of the Kuleshov effect with his experiments between 1910 and 1920 ...<p>In the - now famous - film below, Kuleshov edited a shot of the expressionless face of an actor, which was alternated with various other shots (a bowl of soup, a girl in a coffin, a woman on a divan).<p>- <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=_gGl3LJ7vHc" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=_gGl3LJ7vHc</a><p>When the clip was shown to an audience, they believed that the expression on the protagonist&#x27;s face was different each time he appeared, depending on whether he was &quot;looking at&quot; the bowl of soup, a girl in the coffin, or the woman on the divan, showing an expression of hunger, grief, or desire, respectively.<p>Of course, the footage of actor Ivan Mosjoukine was actually the same shot each time.-<p>The audience even went on to rave about the actors &quot;performance&quot; ... &quot;the heavy pensiveness of his mood over the forgotten soup, [they] were touched and moved by the deep sorrow with which he looked on the dead child, and noted the lust with which he observed the woman&quot;.-<p>The point is that, given the audiovisual medium, a certain degree of manipulation, or &quot;intent&quot; on the part of those creating the work is always to be expected ...<p>... and, also, that audiences bring their own bagage when they view something.-')
