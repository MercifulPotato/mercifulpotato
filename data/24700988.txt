Item(by='codethief', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602009828, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24696451, text='What I don&#x27;t understand is this: The Singularity Theorems <i>already assume</i> the existence of a trapped surface (and by implication, of a marginally outer trapped surface, i.e. an apparent horizon, i.e. a black hole) and <i>then</i> conclude that there must be a singularity inside.<p>Experimentally, though, we know absolutely <i>nothing</i> about this and the recent black hole-related discoveries (gravitational waves, Event Horizon Telescope) can certainly not be seen as a proof of the existence of singularities, either.<p>Now the Nobel Prize committee says that the discovery is actually<p>&gt; “for the discovery that black hole formation is a robust prediction of the general theory of relativity.”<p>But this has virtually nothing to do with the most famous piece of work by Penrose, i.e. the Singularity Theorems.')
