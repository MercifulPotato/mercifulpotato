That&#x27;s about 13.787 * 10^9 * 365 * 24 * 3600 = 4.348 * 10^17 seconds, which (I think?) is a 59-bit number [1]. 10ths of a second will require 62 bits, which is right about at the edge of what a 64-bit signed integer will allow.<p>If you want milliseconds, you&#x27;ll need at least 69 bits. For nanoseconds, you&#x27;ll need at least 89 bits.<p>So you&#x27;ll either need an integer type that&#x27;s wider than what&#x27;s natively supported in most hardware (thus potentially sacrificing performance), or you&#x27;ll have to sacrifice precision.<p>[0]: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Age_of_the_universe" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Age_of_the_universe</a><p>[1]: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.wolframalpha.com&#x2F;input&#x2F;?i=13.787+*+10%5E9+*+365+*+24+*+3600+to+binary" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.wolframalpha.com&#x2F;input&#x2F;?i=13.787+*+10%5E9+*+365+...</a>')
