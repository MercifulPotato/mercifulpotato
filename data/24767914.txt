Item(by='noir_lord', descendants=None, kids=[24768107], score=None, time=1602611375, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24765074, text='If the foam ball you drop is large enough losses from ablation wouldn&#x27;t be a big deal.<p>You can certainly get the speed down enough so that the words impact crater wouldn&#x27;t be in the report, also you smack them into oceans not land (though smacking them into the desert would be an option I guess if you could shed enough speed).<p>For a 10 meter sphere of nickel foam with a density of 2000kg&#x2F;m3 (so about 60% &#x27;air&#x27; by volume) it&#x27;s ~2000 miles per hour[1].<p><a href="http:&#x2F;&#x2F;hyperphysics.phy-astr.gsu.edu&#x2F;hbase&#x2F;airfri2.html#c5" rel="nofollow">http:&#x2F;&#x2F;hyperphysics.phy-astr.gsu.edu&#x2F;hbase&#x2F;airfri2.html#c5</a><p>Fast but not insurmountable.<p>That sphere would be ~8000 tonnes of pure nickel minus ablative losses (which would reduce the impact speed).<p>Of course you&#x27;d want the density to be below 1000KG&#x2F;m3 or it&#x27;d sink.<p>So a 10m sphere at 850kg&#x2F;m3 would have a terminal velocity of ~1300 miles per hour - or about mach 2 and you&#x27;d be picking up about 3500 tonnes of pure nickel bobbing about.<p>I&#x27;d quite like to see that actually (from a good distance away).')
