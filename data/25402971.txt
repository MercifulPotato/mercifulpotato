just plain stupid, use what works and has a stable past...that&#x27;s it.<p>That&#x27;s absolutely a reasonable point of view -- for your use case.<p>From the perspective of a variety of folks, including corporate IT, makers&#x2F;vendors of drop-in appliances and other folks, the changes to the CentOS product will be  disruptive.<p>This is because <i>production</i> servers and appliances generally need to run, for extended periods (measured in <i>years</i>) the application set <i>specifically</i> designed and implemented for the particular set of software versions on a particular OS&#x2F;distro.<p>For many of the above folks, CentOS fit that bill nicely, as it reliably tracked (that is, was slightly <i>behind</i>) a <i>commercial</i> (i.e., you need to purchase a support contract to access updates and support from the vendor) Linux distro (Red Hat Enterprise Linux -- RHEL), but did not require any support contracts or fees above those for hardware, installation and administration.<p>The problems for the above implementation&#x2F;management&#x2F;administration strategy comes out of changes to the CentOS long term support policy.<p>CentOS (with versions 6 and 7) provide(d) a ten year support window for those versions and promised the same for CentOS 8 (released in 2019).<p>Many of those who had chosen CentOS for it&#x27;s free (as in gratis[0]) access to the commercial RHEL platform took CentOS (whose developers&#x2F;maintainers were hired by RedHat[1] to continue that work in 2014) at its word that support for CentOS 8 would also be supported for that same ten year span, updated their environments and applications to the latest version.<p>That promise of support has now been rescinded, as CentOS will now be the gamma&#x2F;release candidate version of RHEL and will <i>lead</i> RHEL slightly rather than trail it.<p>AIUI, there are many reasons for this change to CentOS development, the biggest being that Fedora (the community project that RHEL used as its Beta platform) is now so far ahead (via its rolling release schedule -- for example the RHEL&#x2F;CentOS 8 kernel is v4.18 vs. Fedora 33 kernel v. 5.9 -- more than two years) that it&#x27;s no longer providing that utility.<p>I absolutely understand why some folks are upset, but claims that &quot;CentOS is dead&quot; and the like are rather hyperbolic in my estimation.<p>Disclosure: I have no financial interest in IBM or Red Hat, nor am I a user of CentOS<p>[0] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Gratis_versus_libre"
