Item(by='PeterisP', descendants=None, kids=[25648891], score=None, time=1609841254, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25640187, text='That&#x27;s the whole point - if we look at the consequences of historical major breaches, they generally don&#x27;t result in large number of customers actually leaving the company.<p>And on some level that&#x27;s even appropriate as generally the <i>whole</i> industry is vulnerable, it&#x27;s reasonable to expect that the competitors have just as bad security as the breached company, they just didn&#x27;t get breached because they weren&#x27;t targeted by this particular attacker or because their breaches aren&#x27;t (yet) detected or because they managed to hide and not publicize that breach.<p>The 2013 data breach at Target is a good example of the worst case scenario because it was the first major widely publicised case, all the newer ones had far less effect because people start to understand that the breached company is not unique (like they thought in 2013 about Target) and even then they lost just ~2.5% of customers, and regained the customer confidence after 2-3 quarters.  So even in the worst case scenario, 97.5% customers won&#x27;t drop you, and any effect is short term, as the Target case demonstrates.')
