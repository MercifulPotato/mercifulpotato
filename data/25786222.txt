The correlation appears robust enough to generally use petroleum prices as an indicator of obesity prevalence as measured through BMI.<p>They&#x27;re all pretty careful to note that &quot;the relationship between gasoline prices and physical activity must be empirically determined&quot;.<p>Maybe, for instance, people purchase less food or go out to eat less frequently in high gas-price places. (See <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;onlinelibrary.wiley.com&#x2F;doi&#x2F;abs&#x2F;10.1002&#x2F;j.1550-8528.1999.tb00715.x" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;onlinelibrary.wiley.com&#x2F;doi&#x2F;abs&#x2F;10.1002&#x2F;j.1550-8528....</a>)<p>Maybe people spend less time commuting in high petroleum prices areas, which increases well being and decreases obesity prevalence (see <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;link.springer.com&#x2F;article&#x2F;10.1007&#x2F;s11524-012-9678-6" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;link.springer.com&#x2F;article&#x2F;10.1007&#x2F;s11524-012-9678-6</a>)<p>Maybe petroleum-based fertilizers lead to high obesity (see <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.cambridge.org&#x2F;core&#x2F;journals&#x2F;british-journal-of-nutrition&#x2F;article&#x2F;prospective-association-between-consumption-frequency-of-organic-food-and-body-weight-change-risk-of-overweight-or-obesity-results-from-the-nutrinetsante-study&#x2F;1B800116CA8AFD21D26B6DF877EF7AC1" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.cambridge.org&#x2F;core&#x2F;journals&#x2F;british-journal-of-n...</a>).<p>Maybe the psychological activity of driving affects metabolism (see <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.sciencedirect.com&#x2F;science&#x2F;article&#x2F;pii&#x2F;S0091743515003655" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.sciencedirect.com&#x2F;science&#x2F;article&#x2F;pii&#x2F;S009174351...</a>).<p>Maybe this is all p-hacking and it&#x27;s nonsense.<p>Regardless, if scholarship is to be believed, moving to a place with high gas prices might help you lose weight. Yes, it still sounds nutty to me as well.')
