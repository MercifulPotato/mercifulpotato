Item(by='ethbr0', descendants=None, kids=[24983947], score=None, time=1604377392, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24974227, text='A better way to look at it, for me, is to start with what a vaccine is doing: it&#x27;s <i>not</i> giving you resistance to a pathogen.<p>It&#x27;s triggering your immune system to produce and&#x2F;or store things, the presence of which then confers resistance when subsequently exposed to the actual pathogen.<p>So from that perspective, the next question is, &quot;Do naturally occurring pathogens, presented in a natural manner, produce an optimal triggering themselves?&quot; To which the answer, as you might guess from the messiness of real-world biology, is &quot;No.&quot;<p>It turns out that while amazing our immune systems are also general purpose and adaptable, and such systems can get confused, or go off on a tangent, or focus on the wrong thing, etc etc.<p>Vaccines interact with this system by balancing two things: (1) how do I trigger the strongest, purest immune response that&#x27;s effective on the thing I want to immunize against &amp; (2) how do I avoid triggering such a strong immune response that I convince the body to attack itself?<p>It turns out, with modern biology, we actually <i>can</i> do better than nature on (1). Even if (2) still requires some guesswork (and a <i>lot</i> of testing).')
