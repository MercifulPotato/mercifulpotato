Item(by='rahimnathwani', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602142156, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24707305, text='Assuming you understand the rule (that Monty will always open a door you didn&#x27;t choose, and that door always has a donkey behind it), then one way to understand the game is:<p>You can choose to have:<p>- The most valuable prize that&#x27;s behind door A, or<p>- The most valuable prize that&#x27;s behind doors B or C<p>When you look at it this way, it&#x27;s obvious that you would rather have the most valuable prize from the &#x27;other&#x27; doors, and the way to do that is to switch.<p>Then you don&#x27;t need to think about whether the probabilities change once Monty opens the door with the donkey.<p>If you need convincing, here&#x27;s a simple python script based on the above:<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;gist.github.com&#x2F;rahimnathwani&#x2F;2b6ca328a74b37b952c75d14b6b34a93" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;gist.github.com&#x2F;rahimnathwani&#x2F;2b6ca328a74b37b952c75d...</a><p>If you&#x27;re still thinking about whether the probabilities change, consider whether &#x27;Monty opens a door with a donkey behind it&#x27; is new information. It&#x27;s not because he always does that. And the two doors you picked are fungible&#x2F;identical except for physical position, as both are in the set of doors you didn&#x27;t pick. So which one he opens is irrelevant.')
