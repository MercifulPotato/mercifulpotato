Item(by='closeparen', descendants=None, kids=[24928078], score=None, time=1603952824, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24927415, text='Earlier this year I had the occasion to do some reading on self-esteem, self-confidence, and self-love. I think this critique is a bit divorced from what the psychologists are actually saying.<p>For starters, the &quot;movement&quot; is definitely aware of your dichotomy. The terms of art are <i>contingent</i> and <i>non-contingent</i>. It considers contingent self-esteem to be fragile because real lives contain setbacks. Its position is basically Stoicism: prefer an inner well-being, unperturbed by circumstances. Don&#x27;t get overwhelmed by the ups and downs. It also contains echoes of Christianity: inherent dignity of all life, unconditional acceptance and love of all God&#x27;s creatures.<p>Narcissism is in your relationship to how people perceive you... that would be squarely in the &quot;contingent&quot; category.<p>We might argue that of course a highly successful person feels inadequate; if he could be satisfied at all he would have been satisfied many achievements ago, and stopped there.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Self-esteem#Contingent_vs._non-contingent" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Self-esteem#Contingent_vs._non...</a>')
