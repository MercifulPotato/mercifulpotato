Item(by='nodamage', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602115379, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24707588, text='The problem for Google is that &quot;compilations&quot; in copyright law may be considered copyrightable even if the underlying data being compiled is not. The legal requirement is that the data needs to be &quot;selected, coordinated, or arranged in such a way that the resulting work as a whole constitutes an original work of authorship&quot;. (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bitlaw.com&#x2F;copyright&#x2F;database.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bitlaw.com&#x2F;copyright&#x2F;database.html</a>)<p>As an example, individual recipes are generally not copyrightable, but if you compile a set of recipes and publish them in a cookbook, the cookbook itself would be copyrightable.<p>As applied to Java, even if individual method signatures like <i>max&#x2F;min&#x2F;split&#x2F;replace&#x2F;delete&#x2F;etc</i> might not be copyrightable, the fact that you&#x27;ve organized those specific methods into specific classes named <i>Math&#x2F;String&#x2F;File</i> and then organized those classes into specific packages named <i>java.lang</i> and <i>java.io</i> might meet the threshold for copyrightability as a &quot;compilation&quot;.<p>While it would likely not be good for the software industry for reasons discussed in this thread, I would not be particularly surprised if the court ruled in favor of Oracle on this particular subject.')
