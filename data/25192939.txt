I can&#x27;t even speculate without knowing what you mean by &quot;access to the enclave&quot;.<p>If you mean access as in being able to arbitrarily read and write keys or data to&#x2F;from it, then no, you won&#x27;t be able to access it that way. After all that&#x27;s the whole point - even physical access to the hardware won&#x27;t enable you to extract information (keys, etc.) from it.<p>This means any data or firmware stored on the chip by Microsoft or any OEM (e.g. firmware encryption keys or device signatures) won&#x27;t be accessible to consumers.<p>TPMs define interfaces, though that allow programs to access its capabilities, so there are ways to interact with the hardware (and those are documented as well so you can write applications or operating systems that support it).<p>To get an idea what the interface looks like, you can check out the documentation of the Windows API: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.microsoft.com&#x2F;en-us&#x2F;windows&#x2F;win32&#x2F;secprov&#x2F;win32-tpm" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.microsoft.com&#x2F;en-us&#x2F;windows&#x2F;win32&#x2F;secprov&#x2F;win32...</a><p>There&#x27;s also a list of some TPM commands available on the same site: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.microsoft.com&#x2F;en-us&#x2F;windows&#x2F;win32&#x2F;secprov&#x2F;addblockedcommand-win32-tpm" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.microsoft.com&#x2F;en-us&#x2F;windows&#x2F;win32&#x2F;secprov&#x2F;addbl...</a>')
