He&#x27;s said in the past that (colloquial) <i>Indonesian</i> is one of the most streamlined languages around. (But really English is becoming the world&#x27;s international language.)<p>One of the topics in McWhorter&#x27;s research is about how languages become streamlined when they are learned by adults as a second language. English, Mandarin, and Swahili all show evidence of this (for English, it seems related to the Vikings).<p>Related post:<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.theatlantic.com&#x2F;international&#x2F;archive&#x2F;2016&#x2F;06&#x2F;complex-languages&#x2F;489389&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.theatlantic.com&#x2F;international&#x2F;archive&#x2F;2016&#x2F;06&#x2F;co...</a><p>For people interested in human languages (or people who don&#x27;t yet know they&#x27;re interested in human languages), you might enjoy the courses he&#x27;s done for Great Courses (paid but worth it, available on ~Amazon):<p>(1) The Story of Language: covers the topic of language evolution; how languages change over time, how new words are introduced, how languages mix (due to e.g. invasions), pidgins&#x2F;creoles<p>(2) Language Families of the World: a survey of what languages currently exist, features of languages in different families')
