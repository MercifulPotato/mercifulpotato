Item(by='jfengel', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1608317367, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25469591, text='It was explicitly constitutional. They managed to avoid the word &quot;slave&quot;, but Article 1, Section 2, clause 3 explicitly distinguishes &quot;free Persons&quot; from &quot;all other persons&quot; (who only count for 3&#x2F;5 when it comes to computing representatives).<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Three-fifths_Compromise" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Three-fifths_Compromise</a><p>It also explicitly said that you can&#x27;t escape &quot;service or labor&quot; just by going to a state that didn&#x27;t allow it.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Article_Four_of_the_United_States_Constitution#Clause_3:_Fugitive_Slave_Clause" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Article_Four_of_the_United_Sta...</a><p>They called it the Fugitive Slave Clause, even though the word doesn&#x27;t appear in the Constitution. So yeah, that really wasn&#x27;t just a matter of judges thinking about the &quot;spirit&quot; of the constitution. It was very clear that this was a slave nation.<p>Bonus: it still is, even with the 13th amendment. It <i>does</i> use the word slavery, and then provides an exception for people convicted of a crime.')
