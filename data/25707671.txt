It&#x27;s dissonance <i>as a language</i> - traditional expectations of how the harmony develops being stretched, subverted, warped, and so on. For emotional effect.<p>There isn&#x27;t really a modern equivalent, which is more or less the point. Not even jazz, which is distantly related.<p>But you can get a remote sense from something like Damien Hirst&#x27;s Verity Statue, which starts from some familiar expectations of public sculpture and subverts and undermines them.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=2jrvX2ZrDvk" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=2jrvX2ZrDvk</a><p>The difference is Hirst evokes horror and a Capitalist Gothic aesthetic.<p>Schubert is superficially more reassuring <i>now</i>. But at the time he was influenced by what used to be called the Sublime - which doesn&#x27;t just mean excellent as it does today, but used to mean a complex state of emotion and experience that was so intense and rich it was overwhelming.<p>That&#x27;s what&#x27;s buried in Schubert&#x27;s use of harmony and dissonance.')
