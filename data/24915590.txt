Item(by='wahern', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1603863475, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24913047, text='AFAIU, catching <i>any</i> illness is likely to reduce your vitamin D levels. For one thing, the active metabolites are created by the liver and then the kidneys. <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.cdc.gov&#x2F;nutritionreport&#x2F;99-02&#x2F;pdf&#x2F;nr_ch2b.pdf" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.cdc.gov&#x2F;nutritionreport&#x2F;99-02&#x2F;pdf&#x2F;nr_ch2b.pdf</a> Illness can depress this process. See, e.g. <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov&#x2F;pmc&#x2F;articles&#x2F;PMC6360762&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov&#x2F;pmc&#x2F;articles&#x2F;PMC6360762&#x2F;</a> Furthermore, if you&#x27;re bed-ridden then you&#x27;re not likely getting sunlight, and it only takes a day or two of no sunlight for your serum levels to drop significantly.<p>Of course, none of this is to say that there&#x27;s no reverse causative relationship <i>as</i> <i>well</i>, but people <i>really</i> need to take these correlation observations with a huge grain of salt. And also take note of <i>extremely</i> <i>inconsistent</i> results when controlled trials are attempted.')
