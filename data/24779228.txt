Item(by='rasz', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602696581, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24775691, text='Under Apple ‘Reuse and Recycle’ program contracts stipulate shredding _everything_ to bits.<p>&gt;Li Tong, one of Apple’s contractors in Hong Kong, says that “Shredding components takes more energy than repurposing.” She works and advises her other customers on how to repurpose their discarded tech into new tech, but Apple chooses to discard and destroy all of their components.<p>They even boasted about it in advertisements, and people bought it as environmentally friendly.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;grist.org&#x2F;business-technology&#x2F;apples-recycling-robot-wants-your-old-iphone-dont-give-it-to-him&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;grist.org&#x2F;business-technology&#x2F;apples-recycling-robot...</a><p>&gt;When Apple destroys your old device, plenty of perfectly functional computer processing chips and cameras that could live on ... get melted down. Screens that could have replaced cracked ones, lending a few years of life to an older phone, are pulverized, and the trace amounts of the minerals that make them work are lost as so much dust.<p>&gt;<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;web.archive.org&#x2F;web&#x2F;20160220010603&#x2F;https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bloomberg.com&#x2F;news&#x2F;features&#x2F;2016-02-16&#x2F;where-your-iphone-goes-to-die" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;web.archive.org&#x2F;web&#x2F;20160220010603&#x2F;https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.bloom...</a><p>&gt;The plant is one of a handful around the world, chosen by Apple Inc. to grind up and recycle its iconic phones. And just as the companies that manufacture the handsets are subject to strict standards and secrecy, the same applies in reverse for their disassembly, right down to weighing the shreds, to make sure nothing is lost.<p>&gt;Apple pays for the service and owns every gram, from the used phone at the start to the pile of dust at the end, said Linda Li, chief strategy officer for Li Tong.<p>&quot;pile of dust at the end&quot;. Apple is reusing &quot;reaching 85 percent, by weight&quot; metals used in its products.')
