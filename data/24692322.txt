Item(by='jlokier', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1601933859, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24686027, text='<i>That</i> kind of magic in Perl has a different kind of meaning than &quot;magic number&#x2F;bool&#x2F;string parsing&quot; in YAML.<p>Perl tied-variable magic just means there are (effectively) getter and setter properties attached to the variable.  &quot;Magic&quot; is just the name that was chosen in the implementation, and it stuck.<p>It&#x27;s used to implement variables with special, automatic meanings, like $$ for &quot;current pid&quot; and $! for &quot;last error&quot;.<p>It&#x27;s also used to implement variables with user-defined behaviours on access, which is quite handy for a lot of abstractions.<p>A <i>lot</i> of modern languages support both of these things, because they are useful, but it&#x27;s not called magic in those languages, it&#x27;s called something like &quot;watchers&quot;, &quot;proxies&quot;, &quot;getters and setters&quot; or &quot;hooks&quot;.<p>No, the criticism of YAML-style &quot;magic&quot; is that it leads to entirely surprising behaviour from innocuous input.  Perl magic is not that kind. If you&#x27;re using a special variable, you already know why.')
