I&#x27;m not evaluating my model of the world on its effect on society if made popular, only its proximity to reality and predictive power.<p>I don&#x27;t think understanding that beliefs are arbitrary and fighting for your beliefs is mutually exclusive. If anything there&#x27;s at least the acknowledgement that because many moral arguments work both ways, bringing people who disagree to your position requires other methods.<p>If you want to get more technical, there are normative and positive statements, the former describing what we are talking about here. <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Normative_ethics" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Normative_ethics</a><p>In that lens, &quot;Your stance is what seems to me what would increase extremism&quot; is probably positive because it could be evaluated experimentally, but &quot;cutting in line is wrong&quot; is a normative statement.<p>My open question (and I&#x27;m very open to being wrong) is if there&#x27;s a way to convince line cutters from a culture of line cutters of their wrongdoing without resorting to violence or coercion. How would you do it?')
