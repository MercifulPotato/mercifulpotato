Item(by='leguminous', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1603287105, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24844527, text='I find it fascinating that many of these instruments did not exist in their modern forms until around the same period. Playing clarinet in school, I had never really considered that our instruments weren&#x27;t necessarily the same as the ones a piece had been composed for.<p>* The development of airtight pads for the clarinet in 1812 allowed manufacturers to add more keys, culminating in the modern Boehm system in 1839. The earlier clarinets looked like this: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=D3NCGSvKHCQ" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=D3NCGSvKHCQ</a><p>* Between around 1800-1850, the piano gained the cast iron plate, stronger steel wires, and the double escapement action. 88 keys weren&#x27;t common until the latter part of the century. Earlier pianos (fortepianos) looked like this: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=2ef95BZfYcw" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.youtube.com&#x2F;watch?v=2ef95BZfYcw</a><p>* Antonio de Torres is credited with developing the modern classical guitar starting in the 1850&#x27;s. Steel string guitars were developed later, and were made commercially by Gibson and Martin starting in the 1920&#x27;s.<p>I also played the clarinet in school, took piano lessons at home, and am currently learning guitar. Great choices!')
