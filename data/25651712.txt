Item(by='nostrademons', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1609883863, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25650303, text='I was curious about how this&#x27;d look without the cherry-picking, so I took a look at Wikipedia&#x27;s list of revolutions from the 1900s on and sampled a few dozen:<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;List_of_revolutions_and_rebellions#1900s" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;List_of_revolutions_and_rebell...</a><p>Results from the period of 1900-1910 (19 revolutions; I don&#x27;t have time to do more) is that 12 were outright failures: the revolution was crushed, the leaders executed, oftentimes with significant loss of life for the revolutionaries and nearby civilians.  5 were temporary successes: they led to some reforms or a new government, but the government collapsed within 15 years anyway, leading to either anarchy, dictatorship, or conquest by a foreign power.  2 were an &quot;eventual success&quot; (Young Turk revolution, and revolution in the Kingdom of Poland), where the revolution had modest success but later events achieved &quot;peaceful&quot; (if you can count WW1 &amp; WW2 as peaceful) independence.  1 was a success, the Theriso Revolt that broke Crete away from the Ottoman empire and led to its eventual union with Greece.<p>I&#x27;d come to a bleaker conclusion: most revolutions fail, and lead to the deaths of their leaders and most of the people who support them.  Then of the subset that &quot;succeed&quot; (in the sense of not being crushed), a majority lead to government or lack thereof that is just as bad or worse than what came before.')
