Linux is just the OS kernel. UNIX covers interfaces, from APIs to shells and apps distributed by default, which are way out of the scope of Linux. That&#x27;s the responsibility of Linux distributionsm.<p>And by the way, there are linux distributions that are certified UNIX<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;unix.stackexchange.com&#x2F;questions&#x2F;293396&#x2F;is-there-a-linux-distro-thats-unix-certified" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;unix.stackexchange.com&#x2F;questions&#x2F;293396&#x2F;is-there-a-l...</a><p>Moreover, UNIX is standardized. The drive for standardization was derived from the fact that all vendors of UNIX-like OSes were pushing their quirky vendor-specific things. In fact, even within Linux there is no widely established standard regarding basic interfaces, let alone UNIX.<p>With this in mind, do note that macOS&#x27;s market share is significantly larger than the aggregate market share of Linux-based UNIX-like distributions.')
