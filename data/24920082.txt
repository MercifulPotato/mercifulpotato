Desktop adoption is still exceedingly low.<p>Statscounter shows Linux installs about on par with ChromeOS installs [<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;gs.statcounter.com&#x2F;os-market-share&#x2F;desktop&#x2F;worldwide" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;gs.statcounter.com&#x2F;os-market-share&#x2F;desktop&#x2F;worldwide</a>]. Both are beaten out by the &quot;Unknown&quot; category; there are literally more people running we-can&#x27;t-tell-what OS than a Linux-derivative OS.<p>I think these days, the roadblock isn&#x27;t reliability; it&#x27;s Linux being off-mainstream. Network effect, essentially. Most of the (non-Internet) stuff the average person hears about in tech media and by word-of-mouth is not guaranteed to be available on Linux and is basically <i>always</i> guaranteed to be available on Windows, MacOSX, or both. Linux archs have done a decent job of solving the &quot;Can I see my screen with the latest graphics card&quot; problem but are still behind the curve on the &quot;Can I buy Photoshop off the shelf at Best Buy and run it on my computer&quot; curve.')
