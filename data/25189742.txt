Item(by='dehrmann', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1606155327, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25188771, text='Between 1965 and 1988, the share of women in the labor force rose from 35% to 45% and has held around 45% since then. [1]<p>The added labor could have pushed down the price of labor. Productivity acts as a <i>ceiling</i> for the price of labor, so seem those two track suggests that there was a shortage of labor.<p>While the trend started in the 60&#x27;s, likely in response to social changes in the 60&#x27;s, it could have been accelerated by households struggling with 70&#x27;s stagflation, sending more people to work to overcome stagnating wages. There&#x27;d be something like the paradox of thrift, but for labor, where people work more, but aggregate income doesn&#x27;t change much.<p>Real <i>household</i> income has actually gone up 28% since 1985 [2], so there might be something to viewing this through a household lens, not a wage lens.<p>And as others, and even the link say. there was a <i>lot</i> going on in the 70&#x27;s, so this is hard to tease apart.<p>1: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.dol.gov&#x2F;agencies&#x2F;wb&#x2F;data&#x2F;facts-over-time&#x2F;women-in-the-labor-force#civilian-labor-force-by-sex" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.dol.gov&#x2F;agencies&#x2F;wb&#x2F;data&#x2F;facts-over-time&#x2F;women-i...</a><p>2: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;fred.stlouisfed.org&#x2F;series&#x2F;MEHOINUSA672N" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;fred.stlouisfed.org&#x2F;series&#x2F;MEHOINUSA672N</a>')
