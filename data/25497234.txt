Item(by='rayiner', descendants=None, kids=[25497599], score=None, time=1608572468, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25496594, text='That&#x27;s not true. Medicare can and does negotiate the cost of procedures and doctor&#x27;s services. What the law says is that Medicare can&#x27;t negotiate prescription drug prices. That&#x27;s because Medicare doesn&#x27;t directly pay for drugs, except what&#x27;s used incidentally in the course of covered hospital stays.<p>Medicare didn&#x27;t provide coverage for prescription drugs until the adoption of Part D. That is structured differently than other parts of Medicare. Instead of the government serving as the insurer directly, the government subsidizes premiums for a private Part D insurer. The <i>private insurer</i> can and does negotiate drug prices.<p>The fact that Medicare can&#x27;t negotiate prescript drug prices is often invoked as a talking point, but Medicare prescription drug spending accounts for a relatively small proportion of. overall Medicare spending: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.heritage.org&#x2F;medicare&#x2F;commentary&#x2F;good-and-bad-ideas-the-senates-medicare-drug-bill" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.heritage.org&#x2F;medicare&#x2F;commentary&#x2F;good-and-bad-id...</a> (&quot;Consumer choice and competition have made Part D the rarest of government programs: one in which spending hasn’t spiraled out of control. In fact, government actuaries report that federal general revenue spending on the program was $67.8 billion in 2018—that’s less than what it spent in 2015 ($68.4 billion).&quot;).')
