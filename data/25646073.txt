Another is when the information is costly to gather, and there is a Schelling point for people without the information. For example, we all get in the habit of saying &quot;10 degrees&quot; whenever anyone asks the temperature of any city.<p>Similar, there are cases like &quot;What is the capital of the state of New York?&quot; A Schelling-point answer might easily be New York City, even though the correct answer is Albany. There&#x27;s research on this stuff in the &quot;peer prediction&quot; a.k.a. &quot;information elicitation without verification&quot; literature, e.g. Prelec, Seung, McCoy 2017: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;marketing.wharton.upenn.edu&#x2F;wp-content&#x2F;uploads&#x2F;2017&#x2F;08&#x2F;11-02-2017-McCoy-John-PAPER.pdf" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;marketing.wharton.upenn.edu&#x2F;wp-content&#x2F;uploads&#x2F;2017&#x2F;...</a>')
