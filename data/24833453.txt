Item(by='woodruffw', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1603161519, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24833321, text='&gt; In my mind, Kant&#x27;s categorical imperative could be viewed as either a proposed definition of the good (in which case Moore&#x27;s argument is applicable), or a claim about what actually is good (in which case it escape&#x27;s Moore&#x27;s argument)<p>It’s an academic opinion, but I believe it’s the latter: the very first line in the GMS asserts that the metaethical object <i>itself</i> is the good will: “nothing in the world (or indeed beyond it) can possibly be conceived as good without qualification except for the Good Will.”<p>The CI is the logical consequence (according to Kant) of what the good <i>is</i>. But that distinction is definitely subtle in the context of his normative ethics.')
