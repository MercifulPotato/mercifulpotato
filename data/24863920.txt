As demand for everything — particularly labor — climbed, Congress passed the Stabilization Act of 1942, which allowed the president to freeze wages and salaries for all the nation&#x27;s workers. A day after its passage, President Franklin Roosevelt issued an executive order invoking these powers, which applied to &quot;all forms of direct or indirect remuneration to an employee,&quot; including but not limited to salaries and wages, as well as &quot;bonuses, additional compensation, gifts, commissions, fees.&quot;<p>But there was an exemption of massive proportions slipped into a fateful clause: &quot;insurance and pension benefits&quot; could grow &quot;in a reasonable amount&quot; during the freeze.&quot;</i><p>- <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.chicagotribune.com&#x2F;opinion&#x2F;commentary&#x2F;ct-obamacare-health-care-employers-20170224-story.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.chicagotribune.com&#x2F;opinion&#x2F;commentary&#x2F;ct-obamaca...</a><p>And, relevant to today&#x27;s discussion:<p>&gt;&gt; <i>By slapping corporations with tax rates of 80 or even up to 90 percent on any profits in excess of prewar revenue, Congress all but guaranteed a frenzied search for loopholes.</i><p>Also interesting to note that the change in question wasn&#x27;t a switch from workers paying for their own health insurance to it being paid for by employers. Prior to WW2 less than 10% of Americans had any form of health insurance, by the end of the war it was close to 30%.')
