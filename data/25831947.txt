Item(by='smartbit', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1611052237, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25829228, text='Anthropologist Jean Briggs [0] made baffling observations while studying Inuit [1] collective parenting [2] in the 60s <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;text.npr.org&#x2F;685533353" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;text.npr.org&#x2F;685533353</a><p><i>Briggs quickly realized something remarkable was going on in these families: The adults had an extraordinary ability to control their anger.<p>...<p>She was walking on a stony beach in the Arctic when she saw a young mother playing with her toddler — a little boy about 2 years old. The mom picked up a pebble and said, &quot;&#x27;Hit me! Go on. Hit me harder,&#x27;&quot; Briggs remembered.<p>The boy threw the rock at his mother, and she exclaimed, &quot;Ooooww. That hurts!&quot;<p>Briggs was completely befuddled. The mom seemed to be teaching the child the opposite of what parents want. And her actions seemed to contradict everything Briggs knew about Inuit culture.<p>&quot;I thought, &#x27;What is going on here?&#x27; &quot; Briggs said in the radio interview.<p>Turns out, the mom was executing a powerful parenting tool to teach her child how to control his anger — and one of the most intriguing parenting strategies I&#x27;ve come across.</i><p>[0] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Jean_Briggs" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Jean_Briggs</a><p>[1] <i>Never in Anger: Portrait of an Eskimo Family</i> <a href="http:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.hup.harvard.edu&#x2F;catalog.php?isbn=9780674608283" rel="nofollow">http:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.hup.harvard.edu&#x2F;catalog.php?isbn=9780674608283</a><p>[2] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Inuit#Gender_roles,_marriage,_birth,_and_community" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Inuit#Gender_roles,_marriage,_...</a>')
