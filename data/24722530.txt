There was no law on the books that prevented women from signing mortgages in the 60s, but there was systematic discrimination and discounting or ignoring a woman&#x27;s ability to make good on a loan.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;digitalcommons.law.yale.edu&#x2F;cgi&#x2F;viewcontent.cgi?referer=https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.google.com&#x2F;&amp;httpsredir=1&amp;article=1081&amp;context=yrlsa" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;digitalcommons.law.yale.edu&#x2F;cgi&#x2F;viewcontent.cgi?refe...</a><p>&gt; Part I discusses the extent of discrimination against women in this field. It concluded that such discrimination is wide-spread and takes two forms. First, lenders discount a working wife&#x27;s income when she applies with her husband for a home mortgage. Second, lenders are less likely to make a loan to a single woman than to a man.')
