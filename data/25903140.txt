Item(by='felipehuici', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1611583171, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25902397, text='I suppose the right answer is &quot;it depends&quot;. For most unikernels the answer is no: they are highly specialized, often targeting a single application or language, and built on top of small&#x2F;basic OSes. Some of them have targeted various levels of POSIX compatibility, e.g.,  OSv (<a href="http:&#x2F;&#x2F;osv.io&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">http:&#x2F;&#x2F;osv.io&#x2F;</a>) and HermiTux (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;ssrg-vt.github.io&#x2F;hermitux&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;ssrg-vt.github.io&#x2F;hermitux&#x2F;</a>) though they don&#x27;t always support a large number of syscalls&#x2F;applications. An older project was Rump (largely abandoned), which used the NetBSD kernel to be (mostly) POSIX compatible, though performance wasn&#x27;t great and it&#x27;s largely abandoned. An active project targeting at least partial POSIX compatibility is Unikraft (<a href="http:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.unikraft.org&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">http:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.unikraft.org&#x2F;</a>).')
