Item(by='mturmon', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1606847410, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25268000, text='Now, we use optical scanning telescopes with a wide field of view, and image differencing.<p>Optical instruments have a wide field of view and don&#x27;t have the inherent SNR problems that radar does.  (I.e., using the Sun to illuminate objects is more efficient than doing it yourself.)<p>As a side-effect, there are all kinds of other transient sources that these scanning telescopes pick up, like supernovae, relativistic jets from AGNs (&quot;blazars&quot;), exotic variable stars, etc.  (<a href="http:&#x2F;&#x2F;crts.caltech.edu" rel="nofollow">http:&#x2F;&#x2F;crts.caltech.edu</a>)  It&#x27;s a zoo out there!<p>Radar does play a role in orbit characterization, and recovery of object geometry, once the NEO gets close enough.  But you can get orbits through optical techniques too.<p>The NASA center for near-Earth objects (NEOs) is:  <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;cneos.jpl.nasa.gov&#x2F;about&#x2F;search_program.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;cneos.jpl.nasa.gov&#x2F;about&#x2F;search_program.html</a><p>NEO close approaches to Earth, tabulated: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;cneos.jpl.nasa.gov&#x2F;ca&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;cneos.jpl.nasa.gov&#x2F;ca&#x2F;</a>.  There was a really close one today, actually (a 5-meter object at 0.13 lunar distances).<p>For more about the role of planetary radar in NEOs, search for &quot;radar&quot; in this FAQ:  <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.nasa.gov&#x2F;planetarydefense&#x2F;faq" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.nasa.gov&#x2F;planetarydefense&#x2F;faq</a>')
