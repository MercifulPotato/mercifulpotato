It just doesn&#x27;t work like that. But sure, you can do them perfectly.<p>How?<p>- You provide incentives for attractive alternatives.  \n- You invest in those alternatives and you invest in contextual drivers that make the transition more appealing. For instance, you provide an integrated plans for urban planning that cover different concerns.  \n- You emphasize the benefits of those alternatives on an individual level.  \n- You make a compelling narrative why the status quo is holding the individual back.  \n- You diversify your narrative to to identify &#x2F; appeal with as many different social groups as you have to.<p>That&#x27;s just the content. Then there&#x27;s also your political position. Whether your part of a power elite, or your not, you still need tactics to garner support, whether you like it or not. Alinsky&#x27;s 12 rules for radicals, for instance, is a classic example in that regard.<p>The automative industry or the meat industry didn&#x27;t just happen simply because individual consumers out of their own accord. They also happened because consumers where incentivized to buy in a &#x27;modern&#x27; lifestyle with a heavy focus on cheap and convenience during the 20th century as opposed to more traditional, localized ways of living; and because those industries that supported this cultural shift were directly and indirectly subsidized. For instance, cities got covered in highway infrastructure in the 1950&#x27;s and 1960&#x27;s paid for through public funding and public policy making.<p>Public governance is about figuring out feedback loops in social, economical and political dynamics and finding ways to bend them towards your agenda.<p>Banning cars in city centers is already happening in European cities. The usual argument is to make living in the city more comfortable or make the city more attractive for commerce driven by regional or international tourism (both of which having their own drawbacks and tensions).')
