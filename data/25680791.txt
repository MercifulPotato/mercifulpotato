Item(by='bastawhiz', descendants=None, kids=[25681318], score=None, time=1610073285, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25659272, text='It&#x27;s not just sensitive information that users send, it&#x27;s the information you send back. Your users are implicitly (knowingly or not) allowing their ISP, wifi hotspot, or anyone else who might be snooping to see what they&#x27;re looking at and sell that information, inject ads or malicious code (if you&#x27;re browsing on IE6, lord help you), or worse. I want neither Comcast nor a government nor a credit agency nor an unscrupulous proxy server&#x2F;VPN nor a corporate firewall administrator knowing what&#x27;s on the pages I or my visitors look at. And I don&#x27;t want crapware&#x2F;ads&#x2F;lies jammed into those pages by lord knows who.<p>The problem here is that if someone can intercept your unencrypted traffic, they can also disable the headers that instruct the browser to switch to HTTPS. That means that unless you&#x27;re getting your site preloaded into the browser as requiring HTTPS, folks on modern browsers who hit an unencrypted URL on a malicious network are as good as compromised, and they can not only read the traffic but modify it as well. And unless you disable non-HTTPS traffic, there&#x27;s nothing you can do about it.<p>I would strongly recommend against taking the steps in this article unless you&#x27;re running a site specifically for vintage computing enthusiasts.')
