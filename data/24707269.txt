Item(by='grandchild', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602071499, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24706014, text='So I quickly did a back-of-envelope calculation using [0] for source of mass and diameter of the universe.<p>Then I used the formula given in [1]: r = (2 G M) &#x2F; (c^2).<p><pre><code>    You have: (2 * G * 1.5 * 10^56 grams ) &#x2F; (c^2)\n    You want: lightyears\n            * 2.3548373e+10\n</code></pre>\nSo about 23,548,373,000 ly of SR, while the radius of the observable universe is given as ~46,500,000,000 ly. So the ordinary-matter ratio is about half the density of a black hole. Not quite there, but still _much_ closer than I would have thought!<p>If we account for not just ordinary matter but dark matter (a ratio of 4.9% to 26.8%) the picture is different:<p><pre><code>    You have: (2 * G * (1.5 * 10^56 grams + (1.5&#x2F;4.9*26.8) * 10^56 grams) ) &#x2F; (c^2)\n    You want: lightyears\n            * 1.5234355e+11\n</code></pre>\nSo the SR then would be about 5 times larger. But I have no idea whether dark matter would count towards the black hole mass and SR, and I don&#x27;t know if anyone does.<p>[0] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Observable_universe" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Observable_universe</a><p>[1] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Schwarzschild_radius" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Schwarzschild_radius</a><p>(The scientific calculator I used, which is awesome every day is &quot;units&quot; btw: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;linux.die.net&#x2F;man&#x2F;1&#x2F;units" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;linux.die.net&#x2F;man&#x2F;1&#x2F;units</a>)')
