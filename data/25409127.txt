Item(by='JumpCrisscross', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1607886334, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25408520, text='&gt; <i>What sources do you have to back up this claim?</i><p>The petrodollar hypothesis is a misreading of a historical term. Petrodollar recycling was an American foreign policy priority in the 1970s, and it turbocharged dollar demand [1]. But its role was in supporting, not creating, dollar hegemony. Today it is no longer relevant.<p>At $50 a barrel, the 100 million barrels of oil traded in 2019 and expected to trade in 2021 [2], ~$1.8 trillion a year, is about what the U.S. exports and three quarters of what it imports [3]. Each of these figures pale to the $2 trillion of non-derivative international investments made by America in Q2 2020 alone [4]. (This doesn’t count the trillions more invested by foreigners with dollars.) Or the $10+ trillion of international dollar debt [5].<p>The exports and investments create investable, spendable overseas dollars. The imports and borrowing create dollar demand. Oil is a minority of the latter, one among many commodities.<p>Changes in the dollar don’t affect oil prices [6].
