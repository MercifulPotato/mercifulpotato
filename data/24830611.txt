Item(by='adrianmonk', descendants=None, kids=[24835051, 24833085], score=None, time=1603139234, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24827360, text='On the subject of optimizing tput, this article mentions using hard-coded strings. Another approach (for some tput commands) is to run the command once, save its output in a variable, and re-use it.<p>For example, a slow version of printing a blank chess board:<p><pre><code>   #! &#x2F;bin&#x2F;sh\n   \n   for rowpair in 1 2 3 4\n   do\n     for colpair in 1 2 3 4\n     do\n       echo -n &quot; $(tput rev) $(tput sgr0)&quot;\n     done\n     echo\n   \n     for colpair in 1 2 3 4\n     do\n       echo -n &quot;$(tput rev) $(tput sgr0) &quot;\n     done\n     echo\n   done\n</code></pre>\nAnd a faster version:<p><pre><code>   #! &#x2F;bin&#x2F;sh\n   \n   rev=$(tput rev)\n   sgr0=$(tput sgr0)\n   \n   for rowpair in 1 2 3 4\n   do\n     for colpair in 1 2 3 4\n     do\n       echo -n &quot; $rev $sgr0&quot;\n     done\n     echo\n   \n     for colpair in 1 2 3 4\n     do\n       echo -n &quot;$rev $sgr0 &quot;\n     done\n     echo\n   done\n</code></pre>\nIn many cases, tput is doing doing anything more than looking up a string and printing it. Though in other cases like &quot;tput cols&quot;, it is doing more. (And anyway, the number of columns isn&#x27;t a constant.)')
