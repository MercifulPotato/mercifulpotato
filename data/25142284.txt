The guard rail is not a bad thing, but all in all the process does not seem to lead to actually solving deeper issues.<p>Interestingly, in talk therapy it seems to be the patient&#x27;s own talking that helps, rather than how the therapy is done or who&#x27;s the therapist. Mark Manson sums the research up nicely in this piece:<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;markmanson.net&#x2F;how-to-get-better" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;markmanson.net&#x2F;how-to-get-better</a><p>Turns out that any way to examine and express one&#x27;s thoughts and emotions that otherwise run unattended is helpful. Therapy, journaling, meditation.<p>Still, to me this is by it&#x27;s very nature limited - to what you can consciously dig out and express. If the issues that cause trouble are not conscious, tough luck. Like trying to fix email infrastructure issues by rigorously applying inbox zero.<p>Perhaps lasting improvements in such cases require forms of therapy that involve consciousness altering techniques? Psychedelics are recently making a comeback in therapy. &quot;How to Change Your Mind&quot; by Michael Pollan is on my reading list:<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;How_to_Change_Your_Mind" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;How_to_Change_Your_Mind</a>')
