In legal parlance, that&#x27;s a method-of-operation, and the relevant case law was Seldon(?). In which someone wrote a book on double entry accounting, then sued someone for infringement of their forms. The process of using a two column form couldn&#x27;t be copyrighted, since that would essentially be a patent, and the Court determined a book teaching you how to do a thing implicitly grants license to do so without jeopardy of infringement. API&#x27;s represent the documentation and actuators to make a machine do it&#x27;s thing, therefore, both by test of &quot;is it a control or thing allowing one to control another thing?&quot; (Method-of-operation in common parlance) or &quot;is it a communicative work teaching you to how to operate a specific process?&quot; It passes muster.<p>I thought that was rather straightforward.<p>What wasn&#x27;t straightforward to me was Oracle trying to argue that somehow, it makes sense that everyone should be forced to write their own language runtimes, and that somehow you can separate Java the language and virtual machine from the entity hierarchy, or that that is even a tenable state of affairs. Goldstein should have been slamming the &quot;in Java&quot; point <i>hard</i>.<p>Further, why was no one arguing that programming languages themselves are the front-end manifestations of interoperability by converting high level linguistics down to machine code?<p>This isn&#x27;t hard.')
