I know of at least one other study confirming this result (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;doi.org&#x2F;10.1111&#x2F;j.1444-0938.2006.00055.x" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;doi.org&#x2F;10.1111&#x2F;j.1444-0938.2006.00055.x</a>). This was controversial since it seems to be contrary to results of similar studies on animals.<p>However, in both of those studies, participants wore their under-corrective glasses continually, regardless of whether they were doing near-work or just walking &#x2F; looking at a distance. This is a crucial error, as pointed out by at least Hung and Ciuffreda: &lt;<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.oepf.org&#x2F;sites&#x2F;default&#x2F;files&#x2F;journals&#x2F;jbo-volume-15-issue-3&#x2F;15-3%20Hung-Ciuffreda.pdf" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.oepf.org&#x2F;sites&#x2F;default&#x2F;files&#x2F;journals&#x2F;jbo-volume...</a>&gt;. It makes intuitive sense to me that under-corrective or anti-corrective glasses should only be worn for a limited amount of time each time and only when doing near-work, in order to induce temporary pressure that the (feedback systems of the) eye can then attempt to correct.<p>Also see this 2009 review by Barrett regarding the more general practice of behavioral vision therapy: &lt;<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;doi.org&#x2F;10.1111&#x2F;j.1475-1313.2008.00607.x" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;doi.org&#x2F;10.1111&#x2F;j.1475-1313.2008.00607.x</a>&gt;. Behavioural vision therapy is a wider term, referring not only to the effort to slow myopia, but also to correct other vision problems through changes in behaviour.')
