Item(by='joshuaissac', descendants=None, kids=[25044501, 25036976], score=None, time=1604937602, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25033116, text='&gt; As recently as 15 years ago, using &quot;they&quot; to refer to a single individual was derided by many as grammatically incorrect.<p>&gt; The recent acceptance comes down to two factors<p>Here is a couplet from Shakespeare&#x27;s &quot;A Comedy of Errors&quot; (1623):<p>&gt; There&#x27;s not a man I meet but doth salute me<p>&gt; As if I were their well-acquainted friend<p>And the King James Version (1611) of the Bible has this for Deuteronomy 17:5:<p>&gt; Then shalt thou bring forth that man or that woman, which have committed that wicked thing, unto thy gates, even that man or that woman, and shalt stone them with stones, till they die.<p>And these are not fringe publications, but some of the most widely-distributed texts in the English language.  That makes singular &quot;they&quot; even older than singular &quot;you&quot;! I find it hard to understand the justification for opposing singular &quot;they&quot; even from a prescriptive perspective.')
