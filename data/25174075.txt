Item(by='js2', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1606008293, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25174033, text='From &quot;Down with meritocracy&quot; (Thu 28 Jun 2001 21.59 EDT):<p><i>The book was a satire meant to be a warning (which needless to say has not been heeded) against what might happen to Britain between 1958 and the imagined final revolt against the meritocracy in 2033.<p>Much that was predicted has already come about. It is highly unlikely the prime minister has read the book, but he has caught on to the word without realising the dangers of what he is advocating.<p>Underpinning my argument was a non-controversial historical analysis of what had been happening to society for more than a century before 1958, and most emphatically since the 1870s, when schooling was made compulsory and competitive entry to the civil service became the rule.<p>Until that time status was generally ascribed by birth. But irrespective of people&#x27;s birth, status has gradually become more achievable.<p>It is good sense to appoint individual people to jobs on their merit. It is the opposite when those who are judged to have merit of a particular kind harden into a new social class without room in it for others.<p>Ability of a conventional kind, which used to be distributed between the classes more or less at random, has become much more highly concentrated by the engine of education.<p>...</i><p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.theguardian.com&#x2F;politics&#x2F;2001&#x2F;jun&#x2F;29&#x2F;comment" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.theguardian.com&#x2F;politics&#x2F;2001&#x2F;jun&#x2F;29&#x2F;comment</a>')
