Item(by='jeffbee', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1603393695, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24861748, text='Quick explanation: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.cs.utexas.edu&#x2F;~bornholt&#x2F;post&#x2F;memory-models.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.cs.utexas.edu&#x2F;~bornholt&#x2F;post&#x2F;memory-models.html</a><p>Basically, although I&#x27;m sure someone will shout me down for this, x86 provides something close to sequential consistency among loads and stores even for concurrent programs, whereas ARM and POWER provide almost no kind of order for loads and stores without barriers. High-level languages like C++ do not really hide this problem. It is very easy to write a concurrent C++ program on x86 that appears to work in practice but never works on ARM.')
