Item(by='lstamour', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1611987691, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25965310, text='WebKit Blog was one of the earliest resources to show the benefit of producing or previewing web content both for HDR P3 and for standard sRGB output. Best viewed on an OLED phone: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;webkit.org&#x2F;blog&#x2F;6682&#x2F;improving-color-on-the-web&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;webkit.org&#x2F;blog&#x2F;6682&#x2F;improving-color-on-the-web&#x2F;</a> or <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;webkit.org&#x2F;blog-files&#x2F;color-gamut&#x2F;comparison.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;webkit.org&#x2F;blog-files&#x2F;color-gamut&#x2F;comparison.html</a><p>Recently (2016) YouTube added support for BT.2020 HDR content such as <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;youtube.com&#x2F;playlist?list=PLyqf6gJt7KuGArjMwHmgprtDeY8WDa8YX" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;youtube.com&#x2F;playlist?list=PLyqf6gJt7KuGArjMwHmgprtDe...</a> - you’ll know it’s an HDR video on YouTube by a red symbol that says HDR over the quality control, or if you turn on Stats for Nerds from settings it says bt.2020 in the text somewhere. Even more recently YouTube added live streaming HDR <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;blog.youtube&#x2F;news-and-events&#x2F;seeing-believing-launching-hdr-live-streams&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;blog.youtube&#x2F;news-and-events&#x2F;seeing-believing-launch...</a>  which should come in handy now that PS5 supports HDMI 2.1 and HDR, making the 48” LG OLED TV one of the best gaming monitors ever. (But really expensive and kind of big still...)<p>Unfortunately HDR is backwards compatible, so you have to know your video out and screen are both HDR too. Most flagship OLED cellphones are HDR in P3 colour space if they cost over US$800. Dolby Vision content on Netflix like <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.netflix.com&#x2F;title&#x2F;81017017?s=i&amp;trkid=13747225" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.netflix.com&#x2F;title&#x2F;81017017?s=i&amp;trkid=13747225</a> would also show off HDR. I can’t seem to find evidence in the Stats for Nerds box on iOS that it is indeed playing back an HDR copy of the video. You could also try this app, but it didn’t seem to detect my iPhone as Dolby Vision capable when it was: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;apps.apple.com&#x2F;us&#x2F;app&#x2F;dolby-summit&#x2F;id1528227248" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;apps.apple.com&#x2F;us&#x2F;app&#x2F;dolby-summit&#x2F;id1528227248</a>  Oh— one last thing, for best colour reproduction, turn off Night Shift, True Tone and turn the brightness all the way up. That will give you the maximum range of colours your screen supports from the most dim blacks to the brightest reds, greens and blues on an OLED display...')
