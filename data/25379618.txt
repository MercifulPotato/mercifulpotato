Item(by='kmeisthax', descendants=None, kids=[25380376], score=None, time=1607639079, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25379455, text='So, here&#x27;s the thing: Apple already has this, and has had it for a decade. When large companies want to have an internal app, they go to Apple and buy an enterprise signing certificate, and then you can just download and run the app.<p>Well, <i>almost</i> - there&#x27;s two hitches.<p>1. You have to manually trust the developer before the app will launch, which involves a huge dance of finding the developer trust settings (which are well hidden) and then selecting the developer of the enterprise-signed app\n2. Apple will revoke your account if they catch your apps distributing outside of your organization<p>Technically speaking, there&#x27;s zero changes Apple has to make in order to allow competition in app distribution. It&#x27;s all policy. Apple <i>could</i> implement notarization requirements like they did with macOS, and they should, but that doesn&#x27;t affect the underlying fact that people worried about third-party app distribution are already too late to the party. Here&#x27;s the thing: plenty of developers abuse the system anyway. But it&#x27;s extremely obvious and signposted to the user that they&#x27;re installing a third-party app Apple hasn&#x27;t reviewed. You aren&#x27;t going to just click a link on a Facebook ad and suddenly have malware on your phone.')
