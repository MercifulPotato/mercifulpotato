Item(by='outworlder', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1606181048, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25193859, text='Not really, it&#x27;s really theft.<p>Were it not theft, the property would only be confiscated at the end of legal proceedings, IF it turned out it was related to the crime in question, IF the person was convicted. If those conditions did not hold, the assets should be returned.<p>In actuality, no crime is required for the property to be confiscated. It can be as simple as carrying a &quot;substantial&quot; amount of money in your person during a road trip, and getting pulled over. Where substantial is subjective. Let&#x27;s say 1k.<p>Then your 1000 dollars get prosecuted, in a case titled &quot;United States VS 1000 dollars in 10 dollar bills&quot; and your money has to defend itself. I am not joking.<p>This mechanism was created – supposedly – to empty the coffers of suspected drug dealers in cases police couldn&#x27;t actually prove their guilt. It was designed to be used on people that haven&#x27;t been convicted.<p>That&#x27;s not limited to money. It could be a house. It doesn&#x27;t matter.<p>All it requires is a &#x27;preponderance of evidence&#x27; that the property is related to a crime, which is a low bar. And even if it turns out that the charges are valid, it&#x27;s still not ok.
