From experience I can tell you<p>a) most companies deal with small amounts of data. Small can mean dozens of megabytes to dozens or hundreds of gigabytes. A single well provisioned server will typically be able to handle that very well. Also an SQL database can do a great deal if you know what you&#x27;re doing.<p>b) inappropriately used big data frameworks are expensive performance killers. <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;adamdrake.com&#x2F;command-line-tools-can-be-235x-faster-than-your-hadoop-cluster.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;adamdrake.com&#x2F;command-line-tools-can-be-235x-faster-...</a> for example.<p>c) Good quality programming, as in understanding the machine, memory layout and why it matters, and a good understanding of algorithms (and a hefty dose of common sense), will often yield you more speedup than buying almost any number of new machines.<p>c) Hiring is often driven by fads and companies often don&#x27;t like being told &#x27;you don&#x27;t need this roomful of servers&#x27;, they like to waste money, so maybe do learn them (the profligacy with money is likely to be coming to an end with the economic damage of covid).<p>Takeaway: brainpower will get you much further than horsepower')
