Item(by='lmm', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1601951665, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24692407, text='One part is knowing how important correctness actually is in your case. A lot of people on HN are perfectionists, and there&#x27;s a lot of rhetoric going around our industry about avoiding errors and not breaking things; in reality business processes have always had to be error tolerant (because until recently they were always done by humans) and a quick solution that breaks sometimes is often more valuable than a slow solution that doesn&#x27;t break.<p>Another piece is pervasive auditability. Any result should come with an explanation of where it came from; &quot;Bob did some calculations in his head and he reckons the answer is 7&quot; would be acceptable for some kinds of business decisions, while for others it needs to be more like &quot;Bob followed the procedure specified in the XZY institute handbook, page 456&quot;. Somehow we&#x27;ve let all that go out the window, partly because people who don&#x27;t understand computing are managing organisations that deeply depend on it. But you don&#x27;t even really need computer literacy; what you do need is the same kind of scepticism that you&#x27;d apply to any other piece of work.<p>Managers need to manage. Some of the problem is just people lacking the necessary skills (and a lot of that goes all the way to the top: the UK government doesn&#x27;t have the wherewithal to hire skilled computer professionals because at every level the people on top don&#x27;t have the skills to assess whether the people below them are any good), but a lot is a misplaced perception of computers as infallible.')
