But otherwise, still a good blogpost.<p>------------<p>So if you have 5 data + 1 parity, you now have a 5x6 generator matrix, giving you one extra column for erasures.<p>If one column is erased, you replace the erased column with the parity column, creating a 5x5 matrix.<p>As long as all columns were linearly independent, the resulting 5x5 matrix remains invertable.<p>The specific matrix multiplications &#x2F; inversions &#x2F; operations are well documented in that blogpost.<p>-----------<p>EDIT: Erasure decoding is much easier than error decoding. Error decoding requires figuring out the &quot;locator&quot;, and then applying the calculated errors at those locations. Since we already know the locations in an &quot;erasure&quot; situation, we can just manipulate the matrix in an obvious manner.<p>--------<p>EDIT2: Ah right, the &quot;systematic form&quot; of the Vandemonde-Reed Solomon construction is to perform Gaussian Elimination on the non-systematic matrix (with the goal of forming an identity-sub-matrix). After gaussian elimination, the &quot;column of ones&quot; (or the simple XOR) disappears. (Erm... row of ones in the Backblaze pictures)<p>So maybe the Golay code you&#x27;re thinking of is a better example as a matrix with an explicit parity bit.')
