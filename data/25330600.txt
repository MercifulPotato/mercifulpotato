Contrast Python&#x27;s <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.python.org&#x2F;3&#x2F;library&#x2F;os.html#os.fork" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;docs.python.org&#x2F;3&#x2F;library&#x2F;os.html#os.fork</a> with OpenBSD&#x27;s <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;man.openbsd.org&#x2F;fork.2" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;man.openbsd.org&#x2F;fork.2</a> (or POSIX&#x27; <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;pubs.opengroup.org&#x2F;onlinepubs&#x2F;9699919799&#x2F;functions&#x2F;fork.html" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;pubs.opengroup.org&#x2F;onlinepubs&#x2F;9699919799&#x2F;functions&#x2F;f...</a>). Python&#x27;s solution works reasonably well here - there&#x27;s not <i>that</i> much you can do to handle fork() failing - but os.fork() isn&#x27;t exactly the worst-documented part of the Python standard library either...')
