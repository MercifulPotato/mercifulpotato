Item(by='Evenjos', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1602706605, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=24774762, text='This is one of those overly simplistic views that doesn&#x27;t take the underlying laws and incentives into account.<p>Sure, it&#x27;s bean counters. So then go a step further, and ask yourself: why are the bean counters afraid to take risks on fresh creative content? What market forces are driving them to bet solely on licensed shlock in the post-2000 world? What changed?<p>If the publishing industry is overwhelmed with a flood of writers--it very much is--well, please ask &quot;why.&quot; Dig into the reasons behind the indie author gold rush.<p>There&#x27;s a great in-depth recent history of the publishing industry here: <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.wattpad.com&#x2F;869503247-so-you-want-a-fandom-publishing-marketresearch" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.wattpad.com&#x2F;869503247-so-you-want-a-fandom-publi...</a><p>Quick and dirty summary:<p>Amazon opened its floodgates in 2009, when it offered 70% royalties. Traditional publishing gives authors somewhere around 5% royalties, when all is said and done. That is how the gold rush started.<p>As for publishers betting on tried and true best-sellers... that started earlier, when several laws in the 1980s caused a major consolidation of businesses, including publishers and booksellers. A law got changed, and Barnes &amp; Noble became the arbiter of what got shelf space.<p>There is a lot to this history.')
