Leading with thoughtful praise is probably necessary when making a negative comment <i>if you don&#x27;t already have a trusting relationship</i>.  If it is reasonable for the other person to think you are just armchair-quarterbacking and don&#x27;t actually understand their problem, you need to demonstrate otherwise first, and voicing appreciation for things that they have done is one way to do so.<p>3. If a manager hasn&#x27;t already established this relationship with their direct reports, then there are probably other problems (unless it&#x27;s someone new on the team)<p>4. Having to do this every time will eventually lead to idle flattery or non-sequitur comments just because things don&#x27;t always happen in a nice 2:1 ratio of good to bad.')
