The current decade began when it was 2020 and will end when it’s no longer 202X.<p><a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.npr.org&#x2F;2019&#x2F;12&#x2F;27&#x2F;791546842&#x2F;people-cant-even-agree-on-when-the-decade-ends" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.npr.org&#x2F;2019&#x2F;12&#x2F;27&#x2F;791546842&#x2F;people-cant-even-ag...</a><p>&gt; <i>As Jan. 1, 2020, approaches, it turns out there is a Team Zero and a Team 1 – those who believe the new decade will begin after midnight on the upcoming New Year&#x27;s Eve and those who believe the burgeoning celebrations of a new decade (and all the &quot;last decade&quot; retrospectives) are in fact a year early.</i><p>&gt; <i>In a recent YouGov survey, 64% of Americans said the next decade will begin on Jan. 1, 2020, and end on Dec. 31, 2029. But nearly 20% said they weren&#x27;t sure – and slightly fewer people said the next decade won&#x27;t start until Jan. 1, 2021.</i><p>&gt; <i>On this question, many voices of authority are with the minority.
