While they did believe in a limited government, they also believed in the necessity of effective government, and they did not believe that personal liberty always took precedence.<p>Indeed, that quote you&#x27;re pulling out is almost always misconstrued. Benjamin Franklin was arguing for more taxes to fund a defense against French and Native American attacks on the frontier[0,1]. In other words, <i>for</i> the power of government in the name of the collective and <i>against</i> individual liberty where that liberty threatens society.<p>The same Ben Franklin also became a devout advocate of vaccinations after losing a son to smallpox[1]. He certainly would not have been on the side of anti-vaxxers defying government quarantine or mask orders because &quot;liberty.&quot;<p>Ben Franklin didn&#x27;t even believe in absolute free speech, or that the owners of a &quot;platform&quot; had no right to determine what could or could not be published on that plaform:<p>&quot;In the conduct of my newspaper, I carefully excluded all libelling and personal abuse, which is of late years become so disgraceful to our country. Whenever I was solicited to insert anything of that kind, and the writers pleaded, as they generally did, the liberty of the press, and that a newspaper was like a stage-coach, in which any one who would pay had a right to a place... &quot;<p>...an opinion most of Hacker News would consider Orwellian and fascist.<p>[0]<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.npr.org&#x2F;2015&#x2F;03&#x2F;02&#x2F;390245038&#x2F;ben-franklins-famous-liberty-safety-quote-lost-its-context-in-21st-century" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.npr.org&#x2F;2015&#x2F;03&#x2F;02&#x2F;390245038&#x2F;ben-franklins-famou...</a><p>[1]<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.chop.edu&#x2F;news&#x2F;feature-article-ben-franklin-pro-vaccine-vaccines-were-invented" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.chop.edu&#x2F;news&#x2F;feature-article-ben-franklin-pro-v...</a>')
