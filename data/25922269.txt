Item(by='TeMPOraL', descendants=None, kids=None, score=None, time=1611703599, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25922009, text='Planned obsolescence is a tricky term and has many faces (see e.g. a classification at [0]). A good and widespread example of overt, in-your-face planned obsolescence (surprisingly not mentioned in the article I linked) is &quot;fast fashion&quot;, where nobody even tries to hide that clothing is designed to deteriorate quickly, to accommodate a season-long replacement cycle.<p>I think race to the bottom deserves to be its own type of &quot;planned obsolescence&quot; (again, not mentioned in the Wikipedia classification). While in a highly competitive market, the design process may boil down to &quot;do the same as competitor X, but slightly cheaper&quot; instead of explicitly setting the durability target low, the end result is the same - products that have no business existing enter the market, live very briefly, and forever enter the waste stream. It&#x27;s a systemic problem, and it&#x27;s planned in the sense that if you enter such a market, you&#x27;ve already decided to create short-lived trash.<p>--<p>[0] - <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Planned_obsolescence#Types" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Planned_obsolescence#Types</a>')
