After the Snowden leaks and Shadowbrokers&#x2F;Vault7&#x2F;WannaCry disasters, the agencies put a lot of effort into reassuring the public that US technology was trustworthy. This included things like making public the Vulnerabilities Equities Process [1], and other work to restore trust in cryptographic standards agencies like NIST [2]. It also included more public engagement with industry to report serious vulnerabilities [3].<p>The intelligence community didn&#x27;t open up like this because they wanted to be nice. They did it because there was a very real concern that US industry would be damaged in the eyes of global consumers -- primarily as a result of our intelligence agencies being being too aggressive and, frankly, being sloppy. (It&#x27;s bad enough to pay for and hoard backdoors, it&#x27;s another thing entirely when those backdoors are repeatedly stolen and leaked for bad actors to use.)<p>I guess the news here is that the NSA didn&#x27;t learn very much from these episodes, or at least, it no longer feels like it needs to repair the damage.<p>[1] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Vulnerabilities_Equities_Process" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Vulnerabilities_Equities_Proce...</a>\n[2] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.nist.gov&#x2F;system&#x2F;files&#x2F;documents&#x2F;2017&#x2F;05&#x2F;09&#x2F;VCAT-Report-on-NIST-Cryptographic-Standards-and-Guidelines-Process.pdf" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.nist.gov&#x2F;system&#x2F;files&#x2F;documents&#x2F;2017&#x2F;05&#x2F;09&#x2F;VCAT-...</a>\n[3] <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.thesslstore.com&#x2F;blog&#x2F;nsa-microsoft-releases-patch-to-fix-latest-windows-10-vulnerability&#x2F;" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;www.thesslstore.com&#x2F;blog&#x2F;nsa-microsoft-releases-patc...</a>')
