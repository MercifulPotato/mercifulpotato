Item(by='asciident', descendants=None, kids=[25627829], score=None, time=1609717306, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25626070, text='Interesting idea. But that sounds dystopian. If the scientist declines, then does the university get to publicize that they wanted to release the information but the scientist blocked it (thus casting suspicion on the scientist)? If not, then people wouldn&#x27;t be able to tell if the university asked the scientist for release, or the scientist prevented it, which is still the same problem as before (the public doesn&#x27;t know what happened).<p>Basically, it sounds like an institution at any time can announce &quot;hey we did nothing wrong, and we would like to explain why, and have just asked the employee if they accept but they haven&#x27;t responded yet&quot; while the employee would need to confer with lawyers how to control what now becomes a public spectacle (along with its usual problems of libel, PR, fake news, etc.).')
