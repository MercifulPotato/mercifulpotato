Item(by='rayiner', descendants=None, kids=[25397272], score=None, time=1607736644, title=None, item_type='comment', url=None, parent=25393573, text='The Republican Party has been a fusion of business elite and evangelical Christians since its inception. The two groups not only broadly overlapped, see <a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Quakers" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Quakers</a>, but both had adverse interests to the slaveholding south. The religious segment opposed slavery, and the business segment favored economic policies favorable to manufacturing industry (e.g. a strong currency, tariffs, and infrastructure) versus the weak currency and low tariffs favored by the agricultural south.<p>The strength of each faction waxes and wanes depending on what’s dividing the electorate. During the mid-20th century, both parties embraced “Christian values” (<a href="https:&#x2F;&#x2F;content.gallup.com&#x2F;origin&#x2F;gallupinc&#x2F;GallupSpaces&#x2F;Production&#x2F;Cms&#x2F;POLL&#x2F;ssmrnnozskgngstz2qohsq.png" rel="nofollow">https:&#x2F;&#x2F;content.gallup.com&#x2F;origin&#x2F;gallupinc&#x2F;GallupSpaces&#x2F;Pro...</a>) so economic issues dominated. Since Clinton, the parties are generally both on board with neo-liberalism, so religious and social issues dominate.')
